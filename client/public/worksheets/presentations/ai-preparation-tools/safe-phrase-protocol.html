The most cost-effective defence costs nothing, takes ten minutes to set up, and works against every variant of the attack: a family safe phrase shared in person only and never written digitally. What the attack sounds like§ 01 · adversary view

Most families who have not been targeted treat the safe phrase as theatrical. Most who have been targeted regret not having one. These are the four scripts active criminal infrastructure runs in 2026. Read them aloud once. They're written to bypass exactly the part of you that thinks "this would never work on me."

Script 01 · Family emergency

"Mum? It's me. I'm in trouble. I can't talk long. I crashed the car and I think someone's hurt. I need bail money fast or they'll put me in custody. Don't tell Dad. Please."

Why it works: AI clones your child's voice from a single TikTok or Instagram clip. Crying, panic, urgency, and "don't tell" defeat the brain's verification reflex. Most calls end inside two minutes with a payment demand.

Script 02 · Bank fraud alert

"This is your bank's fraud team. We've blocked a NZ$4,800 transaction. To verify it wasn't you, please confirm your one-time code now or the funds will be released."

Why it works: Spoofed caller ID shows the bank's real number. The voice is professional, calm, slightly impatient. The "code" they want is your actual 2FA code, which they then use to drain the account.

Script 03 · Kidnap hoax

"We have your daughter. You can hear her. Pay $20,000 in the next hour or we kill her. No police. Stay on the line."

Why it works: The "voice in the background" is cloned from social media. The daughter is at school, fine, unreachable for the next 50 minutes. Most parents pay before they verify. NZ Police see this monthly now.

Script 04 · Executive impersonation

"It's [your boss]. I'm in a meeting and can't talk. Need you to wire NZ$45,000 to a vendor right now. Send me the confirmation when it's done. I'll explain later."

Why it works: Voice cloned from a podcast, video call, or town-hall recording. Authority + urgency + plausible deniability. Average loss in this category is six figures. Often arrives during the boss's known travel window.

In every case, the safe phrase ends the call in fifteen seconds. The attacker has no way to know it. The protocol below is what to actually do.

The protocol, in three steps§ 02 · what to do

i.

Choose

Pick 4–7 unrelated words with no common-sense association. Tulip-Anchor-Wrench-Blue style. Avoid:

  • Pet names, child names, school names
  • Anything in your social-media history
  • Any word a household member has said publicly online
  • Birthdays, addresses, common quotes
ii.

Share

Tell every household member over 10. In person only. Never:

  • Texts, emails, cloud notes, password managers
  • Voice messages or video calls (recorded)
  • Any device that has a search function
  • Written down for "in case I forget"
iii.

Use

Required for any phone or video call asking for money, location, or sensitive action. If the caller:

  • Dodges the request — hang up
  • Becomes hostile — hang up
  • Redirects to urgency — hang up
  • Then call back on the saved number to verify

Four hard rules that make the protocol work

The phrase never goes digital. Not in iCloud Notes, not in 1Password, not in a safe deposit box with the kids' names on it. Recall is the failure point under stress; if you can't remember, hang up and call the saved number instead.
No exceptions, no apologies. Banks don't mind being asked. Real family members will understand. If someone is offended that you ask for the phrase, that's information.
Practice quarterly. Real-time recall under stress is harder than you think. Drill it once when you set it up, then again at every quarterly audit.
Replace it if it leaks. If the phrase has been said in front of someone outside the immediate household, in a recorded meeting, or written down even temporarily, replace it. The setup cost is 10 minutes.

Build yours

Pick four to seven unrelated words. The unrelated part matters: the brain wants to make sense of a phrase, which makes it easier for an attacker to guess and easier for you to leak by association. Random nouns and verbs. Imagery you can hold in your head.

These boxes are not saved anywhere. Once you have memorised your phrase and shared it in person with everyone who needs it, clear this row. The phrase does not live on this page.

The conversation with your parents§ 03 · highest-leverage 30 minutes

Why this conversation matters more than you think

Adults aged 60+ lost US$7.7 billion to fraud in 2025 — up roughly 60% on 2024. Voice cloning is a substantial driver. AARP estimates one in five Americans 65+ has been targeted by a voice-cloning scam, and one in ten has fallen for it. Your parents are the highest-leverage people in your network for this protocol. The conversation is awkward. Have it anyway.

You · sitting at their kitchen table, in person

"Mum, Dad — I want to spend ten minutes on something that could save you a lot of money and a lot of stress. There's a kind of phone scam now that's getting really good. Someone calls pretending to be me or [grandchild], using a voice that sounds exactly like mine, asking for money in an emergency. People are losing thousands of dollars to this every week. I want us to set up a simple way to know it's actually me."

"It's just a few words we'll all remember. If anyone ever calls you, even if it sounds exactly like me, asking for money or to do anything urgent — you ask them to say the words. I'll say them. A scammer won't be able to. If they get angry or change the subject, hang up and call my normal number to check."

"Let's pick the words now. Something that has nothing to do with our family — random words. We'll all remember them. We won't write them down anywhere. We won't tell anyone outside our household. Okay?"

If they push back ("I'd know your voice," "I'd never fall for that"): Don't argue. Say: "I know — and probably you wouldn't. This is for the one time someone catches you tired or stressed. It's a backup. It costs us five minutes." Most pushback dissolves when framed as backup rather than capability assessment. If they're worried about remembering: Practice three times in the kitchen. Don't write it down. Recall improves rapidly with even brief practice. Follow up with one practice phone call a week later, and one every few months.
