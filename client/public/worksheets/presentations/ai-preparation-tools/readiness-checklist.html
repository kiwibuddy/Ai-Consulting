The Household Readiness Checklist

A complete reference for resilient households, organised by how often you need to do it.

If the 30-Day Onboarding Plan is the guided sequence, this is the comprehensive reference. The actions are organised by time horizon rather than threat level: the things you do this weekend, this month, every quarter, every year. A household that holds to this rhythm is doing roughly 80% of the resilience work the framework recommends, regardless of which level the world finds itself in.

i. This weekend One-time setup · ~6–8 hours total
Identity
  • Hardware-key 2FA on three accounts. Email, bank, government ID.Two keys: primary + backup, stored separately.
  • Move family chats to Signal. Free, end-to-end encrypted, no metadata harvesting.
  • Choose a family safe phrase. 4–7 unrelated words, in person only, never digital. See Worksheet 03.
Devices
  • Encrypt every laptop hard drive. BitLocker (Windows), FileVault (Mac).Built in. Free. ~30 minutes per machine.
  • Disable phone advertising IDs and unnecessary location services.
  • Clear AI-assistant memory in every tool. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Siri, Alexa.
Financial first-aid
  • Assemble the EFFAK kit. Photo IDs, birth certificates, insurance, prescriptions in waterproof storage.See Worksheet 04.
  • Cash reserve in low denominations. NZ$5, $10, $20 notes for ATM-down scenarios.
Family
  • Tell every family member over 10 about the safe phrase, in person. Practice once.
  • Schedule the elderly-parents conversation. Bring Worksheet 03.
ii. This month Build out · ~4–6 hours total over 30 days
Information ecosystem
  • Build a written list of 20 trusted human sources. Named journalists, researchers, government officials.
  • Reduce passive social-media intake. Time limits, remove apps from home screen.
  • Audit your own voice and video footprint online.3 seconds is enough for cloning.
Local network
  • Map 5 people in walking distance, 10 in your neighbourhood. Note skills.
  • Make actual contact with one of them this month. Coffee, text, knock.
  • Find your local Civil Defence group online.NZ: civildefence.govt.nz to find your CDEM Group.
Civil-defence baseline
  • Standard 7-day household emergency supplies. Water (21 L/person), food, medications, torch, battery radio, first aid.
  • Build to 14-day Stay-at-Home reserve over time.
  • Pack a separate 3-day Go-Kit for evacuation.
Civic
  • Identify one venue where AI policy is being shaped locally. School, council, denomination, professional body.
  • Write the submission, letter, or email. Send it.
iii. Every quarter Maintenance audit · ~1 hour every 3 months
Audits
  • Clear AI-assistant memory across all tools. Quarterly recurring reminder.
  • Review password manager. Rotate any reused passwords.
  • Update the trusted-sources list. Add new ones, drop ones that have lost credibility.
  • Check supply-kit expiry dates. Water, food, medications.
Practice
  • Family safe-phrase recall drill.Recall is the failure point under stress.
  • Test every manual override in the house. Front door key, water main, electrical breaker, garage release. See Worksheet 04.
  • One household conversation about a "what if X" scenario.

iv. Annual review Once a year · ~3 hours · pick a fixed date
Financial
  • Review reserves. 3–6 months baseline; consider building to 12 months at higher threat levels.
  • Insurance review. Add identity-theft and cyber-fraud rider where available.
  • Review concentration risk. Avoid heavy bets on AI stocks in either direction. Diversify across institutions.
  • If you own your home, review options for principal reduction. Paid-off shelter is the highest-value durable resilience asset most households can hold.
Career & vocation
  • Review hybrid-skill plan. Are you building skills with embodied / relational / judgment components AI cannot easily replicate?
  • Audit one income stream not dependent on a single employer. Consulting, teaching, side practice, craft.
  • Cultivate verifiable reputation. Named recommendations, repeat clients, public work product.
Children & adolescents
  • Review family AI policy by age group. Under-12: parent-supervised only. 12–16: specific tasks, no AI companions. 16–18: increasingly autonomous.
  • Audit screen time. Significant unmediated time daily, daily reading of physical books, family meals without screens.
Practice & community
  • Annual contemplative retreat or extended walking time. Re-orient.
  • Review your local network. Add 2–3 new connections.
  • Review one substantive AI-policy engagement done in the past year. Plan the next.

The ongoing rhythm, in one sentence: daily contemplative practice, weekly Sabbath, monthly local connection, quarterly audit, annual review. Every level of the framework — from the calmest Level 1 to the worst Level 5 — points to the same underlying disciplines. The household that has cultivated relational density, embodied practice, and intergenerational depth fares better at every level than one that has not.

What this checklist is deliberately not

This is household-level readiness. It is not a substitute for civil-defence guidance during an actual emergency, where you should follow the directions of NEMA / FEMA / your jurisdiction's emergency-management agency precisely.

This is also not a survivalist document. The actions here are what every credible emergency-management agency in the developed world recommends. The framework explicitly rejects bunker-style preparation, off-grid relocation that severs you from existing relationships, and stockpiling of weapons or speculative assets. The empirical record on those is bad.
