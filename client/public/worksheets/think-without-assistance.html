The spiritual warning
"Falling asleep at the wheel"
Research on AI-assisted decision making found that people free-ride when AI quality is high — putting in less cognitive effort, not more. We are at risk of outsourcing the very thinking that makes us human.
Fabrizio Dell'Acqua — "Falling Asleep at the Wheel," Harvard Business School

"If any of you lacks wisdom, you should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to you."
James 1:5 · The first place wisdom is meant to come from is not a search engine

Ray Miller, writing in The Christian Post, warns that "the interactivity AI brings will feel more like a personal deity that seems all-knowing." That's not a metaphor. It's a description of what happens when your instinct is to ask AI before you've asked God — or even before you've sat with the question long enough to know what you're actually asking.

This worksheet is not anti-technology. It's pro-wisdom. There is a difference between using AI as a tool and outsourcing your thinking, your discernment, and your capacity to hear from God.

The One-Week Log
Track it honestly for seven days
Each day, log one significant question, decision, or moment of uncertainty you faced. Then be honest about where you went first — and what happened.
The "went to first" column is the one that matters most. Not what you eventually did — what your first instinct was before you had a chance to think or pray. That reflex is data.

Part 2
What the Log Reveals
Look at the week as a whole. Patterns are more honest than individual moments.
The reflex pattern
What was your body reaching for before your mind had a chance to engage? That reflex has been trained into you over years.
The avoidance pattern
Were there questions you outsourced not because you needed an answer, but because sitting with the uncertainty was uncomfortable?
The prayer gap
James 1:5 says to ask God for wisdom. How many times this week did you ask Him before you asked anything else?
Part 3
The Harder Questions
1
Is there a question or decision this week where the AI or Google answer was faster — but not actually better?
Speed and wisdom are not the same thing. A quick answer can close off the very uncertainty that God was using to draw you closer.
2
What would have happened if you had stayed with one of this week's questions long enough to actually think and pray about it — without assistance?
Not faster. Longer. What might you have discovered?
3
Dallas Willard listed silence among the core spiritual disciplines. When was the last time you practised it? What makes silence harder than scrolling?
Silence is where God has historically spoken. We have systematically eliminated it.

The question this whole worksheet has been building toward
"What does it mean to sit with a question long enough to hear from God?"
Not long enough to feel comfortable. Not long enough to manufacture an answer. Long enough that the noise settles, the urgency passes, and you are genuinely available to whatever God might say — even if it's silence, even if it's something you don't want to hear.

Dallas Willard said that the disciplines cultivate a disposition of "openness, willingness, and dependence on God." You can't cultivate that disposition while simultaneously outsourcing every difficult question to an AI that responds in three seconds. The capability to think, wait, and listen is a spiritual muscle. Like any muscle, it atrophies when you stop using it.

This is what's at stake. Not just your productivity or your critical thinking skills — though those matter too. What's at stake is your capacity to hear from the God who speaks in still small voices, who draws near in the waiting, who does some of his deepest work in the uncomfortable space of not-yet-knowing.
