Section 1 · Context
The conversation most employers are avoiding
Understanding what's actually happening to work in our region before you talk to your team about it.

The Tauranga subregion sits at 24.7% knowledge-intensive employment against a national average of 32.1%. That gap matters here because it means a higher proportion of local jobs involve tasks that AI is actively reshaping — administration, data processing, customer service, scheduling, and written communication. These aren't abstract statistics for a business owner with five staff.

The instinct for most employers is to wait. Wait until it's clearer. Wait until it directly affects the business. The problem is that your team isn't waiting. They're already reading about it, talking about it at home, and wondering whether you know something you're not saying. The conversation happens either with you or without you.

What this doesn't mean
None of this means you need to restructure your team or start issuing AI readiness assessments. It means having one honest conversation where you tell your team what you know, acknowledge what you don't, and ask them what they're seeing. That conversation is what this worksheet prepares you for.
Step 1.2
Check your own position before you talk to your team

Before any conversation with staff, it helps to be clear about where you actually stand as the employer. Two questions worth sitting with:

Your honest answer
Do you have any concerns about how AI might affect roles in your business over the next two to three years? Be specific.
What you want from the conversation
What do you want your team to understand or feel after this conversation? What outcome would make it worthwhile?

Section 2 · Role mapping
Which roles in your business are most exposed?
A practical guide to assessing AI exposure across common Bay of Plenty small business roles — without the corporate jargon.

"AI exposure" doesn't mean a role is at risk of disappearing. It means a meaningful portion of what someone does in that role involves tasks that AI tools can now assist with or automate. High exposure can mean more efficiency and less drudge work — or it can mean a role needs to evolve. Usually both.

Section 3 · The conversation
What to actually say in the room
A practical script and conversation guide. Not word-for-word — you know your team better than this worksheet does. But close enough to be useful.

The biggest mistake employers make in this conversation is over-preparing the business case and under-preparing for the human reaction. Your team will have two questions before they have any others: Is my job safe? And why are you telling me this now? Answer those early and honestly, and the rest of the conversation gets easier.

Step 3.1
Use this as your conversation guide
A conversation guide for small employers
Adapt this to your voice. The structure matters more than the specific wording.
Opening — you
I want to have an honest conversation about AI and what it means for how we work. I don't have all the answers. What I do know is that it's changing faster than I expected, and you deserve to hear what I'm thinking — not just read about it in the news.
Why this works: It positions you as informed but not omniscient. It signals respect before it signals concern.
On job security — you
I'm not planning any redundancies. What I am thinking about is how our roles might evolve over the next couple of years. Some of the repetitive work — things like [name a specific example from your business] — might get handled differently. I'd rather plan that with you than have it happen to you.
Name a real example. Abstract statements don't land. "Like the weekly report that takes two hours to format" lands better than "administrative tasks."
On upskilling — you
I want to support anyone here who wants to learn how to use these tools. We don't need to do it all at once. But if there's something specific that would make your work easier, I'm open to finding time and budget for it.
Don't over-promise. "Open to finding time and budget" is more honest than "we're investing in full training programmes."
Open the floor
What are you already noticing? Are there things in your role that feel repetitive or time-consuming that you've wondered about? I'd rather hear that than find out later you've been sitting on it.
This is the most important part. Most team members have already observed things worth knowing. Ask and then actually listen.
Common responses — and how to handle them
"Is AI going to take my job?" — "Not as far as I can see. What I think will happen is that parts of your job change. The goal is to make that something we do together, not something that surprises you."

"I don't know anything about AI." — "That's fine. Neither did I six months ago. We'll work through it at a pace that makes sense."

"I already use it a bit." — "Tell me more. What have you found useful?" This person is your best internal resource. Use them.
Step 3.2
Handle the Māori workforce dimension with care

For many Bay of Plenty employers, this conversation will include Māori staff — and the broader context matters here in ways that aren't always obvious to Pākehā employers.

Context worth understanding
The Toi Kai Rawa Trust's Toi Ki Tua programme exists specifically to bridge the gap between Māori tertiary graduates and knowledge-intensive roles in the Bay of Plenty — including AI-adjacent positions at companies like Zespri. This reflects a real aspiration within the region's Māori community to participate in and lead the knowledge economy, not be displaced by it.

If you have Māori staff who are nervous about AI, it's worth knowing that fear of displacement is well-founded historically — and your words in that room carry weight that goes beyond the immediate conversation. Honest acknowledgment of that history, and genuine commitment to developing Māori staff rather than just managing them, makes a real difference.

If you're unsure how to navigate this in your specific context, Priority One and the Toi Kai Rawa Trust are the most relevant local contacts.

Section 4 · Upskilling
What upskilling actually looks like for a small team
You don't need a training budget or an L&D department. You need to know which resources are genuinely useful and which are just marketing.

The honest truth about AI upskilling for small business teams is that most of the value comes from two things: someone spending 30 minutes experimenting with a tool on a real task, and a team culture where it's safe to share what they learn. Formal training helps, but it's downstream of those two things.

Step 4.2
Build a simple AI policy for your team

A team AI policy doesn't need to be a formal document. It needs to answer four questions clearly enough that your team knows what's expected. Fill in your answers below.

Our team AI policy
Four questions. Plain language. One page maximum when you write it up.
Question 1
Which AI tools are we okay with staff using for work tasks?
Question 2
What information should never go into an AI tool?
Question 3
What do we do with AI-generated work before it goes to a client or goes public?
Question 4
How do team members raise concerns or share things they've learned about AI?
Once you've filled this in
Share it with your team as a draft, not a finished policy. Ask for their input before you finalise it. A policy your team helped shape is one they'll actually follow.

Section 5 · Next steps
What to do in the next two weeks
Three concrete actions that cover the most important ground for a Bay of Plenty employer right now.
Step 5.1
Commit to three specific actions
Action 1 — This week
Have a 15-minute version of the conversation in Section 3 with your team. It doesn't need to be a formal meeting. It needs to be honest. Even a brief "I've been thinking about AI and I want to share where I'm at" signals that you're paying attention and that they can too.
Action 2 — This week
Identify one team member who's already curious about AI and give them space to explore it. Ask them to spend 30 minutes on a real work task using Claude or ChatGPT and report back what they found. This costs nothing and tells you more than most assessments.
Action 3 — This month
Write a one-page AI policy using the builder in Section 4. Share the draft with your team before finalising it. It doesn't need legal review for a first version. It needs to answer the four questions clearly.
One thing to avoid
Don't use AI adoption as a cover for decisions you've already made about headcount. If you're planning to reduce a role and AI is part of the reason, that's a different conversation from this one — and your team will know the difference. Handle those conversations separately and with appropriate employment advice. employment.govt.nz has guidance on redundancy obligations.
