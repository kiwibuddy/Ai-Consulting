Section 1 · Context
The gap most small businesses don't know exists
Understanding the landscape before you look at your own obligations.

When you sign up for a free AI tool and start pasting customer information into it, you're making a decision with legal implications — whether you know it or not. Most of the business owners I've spoken to across the Bay of Plenty aren't doing anything reckless. They just haven't had anyone explain the basics in plain language.

This worksheet covers two distinct areas. Privacy law — which governs how you handle customer data when using AI tools. And copyright — which affects who owns what when AI creates something for your business. They're separate issues with separate rules.

Step 1.1
Understand what changed in 2025

NZ's Privacy Commissioner released updated AI-specific guidance in 2025. The core framework isn't new — the 13 Privacy Principles in the Privacy Act 2020 have always applied. What changed is that the OPC now provides explicit guidance on how those principles apply when you're using AI tools that process personal information.

13
principles
The Privacy Act 2020 contains 13 Information Privacy Principles that apply to any organisation collecting, storing, using, or sharing personal information — including through AI tools. They apply to sole traders and small businesses, not just large corporations.
Source: Office of the Privacy Commissioner NZ, privacy.org.nz
What this is not
This worksheet is not legal advice. It's a plain-language overview to help you understand the landscape and ask the right questions. If you have specific concerns about your business's obligations, talk to a lawyer or contact the OPC directly at privacy.org.nz.
Step 1.2
Know the two questions this worksheet answers

Everything in this worksheet comes back to two practical questions:

Question 1 — Privacy
Am I handling customer data in a way that complies with the Privacy Act when I use AI tools?
Question 2 — Copyright
Who owns the content AI creates for my business, and am I allowed to use it commercially?

Both are answerable. Neither requires a law degree. Let's work through them in order.

Section 2 · Privacy law
The Privacy Act and your AI tools
Which of the 13 Privacy Principles you actually need to think about as a small business using AI — in plain language.

You don't need to memorise all 13 principles. You need to know which ones are most likely to be triggered by how small businesses actually use AI tools today.

Step 2.1
Review the principles most relevant to AI use

These are the principles most likely to affect a small business using AI tools. The flag on each shows its typical risk level for AI use.

PP 1
Purpose of collection
Collect personal info only for a lawful purpose directly related to your business activity.
Watch
PP 2
Source of collection
Collect directly from the person where possible. Tell them you're collecting it and why.
Lower risk
PP 3
Collection from subject
Tell the person what you're collecting, why, and who will have access — including AI tools.
High relevance
PP 5
Storage and security
Take reasonable steps to protect personal info from loss, access, or disclosure. Includes third-party AI tools.
High relevance
PP 6
Access to personal info
Individuals have the right to access their own information held by you — including if it's been processed by AI.
Watch
PP 8
Accuracy
Take reasonable steps to ensure the info you hold is accurate. AI outputs about individuals must be reviewed before acting on them.
High relevance
PP 10
Limits on use
Only use information for the purpose it was collected. Don't feed customer data into AI tools for unrelated purposes.
High relevance
PP 11
Limits on disclosure
Don't share personal info with third parties — including AI tools — without a lawful basis. Free AI tools may be considered disclosure.
High relevance
The practical takeaway
The four principles most relevant to AI use are PP3, PP5, PP10, and PP11. Collectively they say: tell people if AI is involved in processing their data, keep that data secure, only use it for the purpose you collected it for, and treat sending data to a free AI tool as sharing it with a third party.
Step 2.2
Understand what a Privacy Impact Assessment actually is

The OPC's 2025 AI guidance recommends completing a Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA) before deploying AI in any customer-facing context. For a small business, this sounds more complicated than it is.

What a PIA actually is for a small business
A PIA is a structured set of questions you ask yourself before adding a new AI tool. For a small business, it doesn't need to be a formal document. It needs to cover: what personal data will this tool touch, where will that data go, how is it secured, and do my customers know?

The checklist in Section 4 of this worksheet is a simplified PIA. Work through it before adding any new AI tool that touches customer data.

💬 Scenario: Using ChatGPT to draft customer emails Review needed ⌄
What's the risk? If you paste a customer's name, contact details, or account information into a free ChatGPT account, that data may be used to train OpenAI's models. This is likely a disclosure under PP11 and may not meet your obligations under PP5.

What to do: Use ChatGPT with placeholders instead of real names. For example: "Write a follow-up email to a client who hasn't responded in a week" rather than pasting their actual details. Or use a business-tier account (ChatGPT Teams or Enterprise) which has data handling agreements.
📊 Scenario: Uploading a customer spreadsheet to an AI tool High risk ⌄
What's the risk? Uploading a spreadsheet containing customer names, contact details, or purchase history to a free AI tool almost certainly violates PP5 and PP11. You're sending personal data to an overseas server with no data processing agreement in place.

What to do: Either anonymise the data before uploading (replace names with IDs, remove contact details), use a business-tier tool with a data processing agreement, or don't use AI for this task unless you've confirmed the tool's data handling policy in writing.
📝 Scenario: Using AI to summarise a contract or supplier document Generally fine ⌄
Why this is lower risk: Summarising a supplier agreement or industry document that doesn't contain personal information about individuals isn't a privacy issue. The Privacy Act applies to personal information about identifiable individuals — not commercial contracts.

Still worth checking: If the contract contains personal details of specific people (e.g. contact names, personal guarantors), treat those details with the same caution as any other personal data.
🤖 Scenario: Adding a chatbot to your website Review required ⌄
What's the risk? A customer-facing AI chatbot collects information directly from members of the public. PP3 requires you to tell them what you're collecting, why, and who has access to it — including the AI provider. If the chatbot makes decisions or recommendations, PP8 (accuracy) and human oversight requirements also apply.

What to do: Add a clear disclosure to the chatbot interface ("This chat is powered by [tool]. Your conversation may be processed by [provider]."). Get a Data Processing Agreement with the provider before going live. The OPC recommends senior leadership sign off on customer-facing AI deployments.
Step 2.3
Note the Māori data sovereignty dimension

With 44% of the Bay of Plenty's future workforce being Māori, and a significant portion of the region's business community serving Māori customers and communities, this is worth understanding — not just as a compliance matter, but as a relational one.

Māori Data Sovereignty
Māori data — information about Māori individuals, whānau, hapū, and iwi — is considered a taonga under te Tiriti o Waitangi. The principle of Māori data sovereignty holds that Māori should have governance over how their data is collected, stored, and used. This isn't just an aspiration: the OPC's AI guidance specifically references the need to consult with Māori where AI systems may affect Māori communities. Local organisations like Toi Kai Rawa Trust can provide guidance on operating respectfully in this space.

Section 3 · Copyright
Who owns what AI creates?
NZ has an unusual legal position on AI-generated content. Here's what it means for your business in plain language.

When you use AI to write a blog post, design a logo, or create marketing copy — who owns the result? In most countries, the answer is murky. In New Zealand, there's actually a specific provision in the Copyright Act 1994 that addresses it, though "addresses it" is doing some heavy lifting there.

Step 3.1
Understand NZ's position on computer-generated works
Situation
What NZ law says
You write something with AI assistance
You are the author. Your creative input (the prompts, editing, selection) establishes your authorship. Standard copyright applies.
AI generates something with minimal human input
NZ's Copyright Act 1994 has a provision for "computer-generated works" with no human author. Copyright still exists, but it belongs to the person who "made the arrangements" for the work — likely the person or business using the tool. This must pass an "objective originality" test.
You use AI to recreate something copyrighted
You're still liable. Asking AI to "write something like [copyrighted work]" or recreate copyrighted material doesn't transfer the legal risk to the AI provider. The risk stays with you.
AI tool's own terms on your content
Most AI tools (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) assign output ownership to the user in their terms of service. But this is contractual — not statutory. Read the terms for any tool you use commercially.
Using AI output commercially
Generally permitted under most AI tool terms. Check each tool. Some require attribution or have restrictions on certain content types.
The honest summary
NZ law is ahead of most countries in having a statutory framework for computer-generated works, but it's still developing. The practical guidance from legal experts like Bell Gully and AJ Park is consistent: the more creative human input you put in, the clearer your ownership is. Don't use AI to replicate existing works. Document your process if you're creating something commercially significant.
Step 3.2
Know the three things that actually matter for your business
Copyright point 1
AI-generated marketing content you create for your business is almost certainly usable commercially. The tool's terms of service typically grant you ownership of outputs. Keep a record of how you created it.
Copyright point 2
Don't use AI to replicate someone else's work. "Make this sound like [specific author/brand]" is lower risk than "copy this paragraph and rewrite it slightly." When in doubt, ask your AI tool to create something original on a topic rather than rewriting existing material.
Copyright point 3
Images are higher risk than text. AI image generators may produce outputs that infringe on the training data used to build them. For business-critical or publicly visible imagery, use stock photo services or commission human-created work where the IP chain is clear. This is an evolving area — legal clarity is still developing globally and in NZ.

Section 4 · Before you add a tool
The five-minute check you should run every time
Work through this checklist before adding any new AI tool that touches customer data or creates content for your business.

This isn't meant to slow you down. It's meant to catch the 20% of situations where something genuinely needs to be thought through before you click "sign up." Most tools will sail through this in five minutes. Some will prompt a useful question.

Step 4.1
Work through the pre-deployment checklist

Tick each item as you complete it. The checklist saves your progress in this browser.

Item Risk level
Does this tool touch personal information about identifiable customers or staff? If yes, the Privacy Act applies. Move through the rest of this checklist carefully.
Must check
Have you read this tool's privacy policy and data handling terms? Specifically: does it use your inputs to train its models, and where is your data stored? Free tools often store data on overseas servers.
Must check
If this tool will be customer-facing, do you have a disclosure in place? Your customers should know if AI is processing information they share with your business. A short sentence in your privacy policy or at the point of interaction covers this.
Must check
Are you using this tool for tasks that could significantly affect individuals? Credit decisions, health assessments, hiring, or anything where an error has real consequences requires human review before acting on AI outputs. This is explicit in OPC guidance.
Must check
If this tool generates content for your business, have you checked the tool's terms on commercial use? Most major tools permit commercial use — but check. Some restrict certain content types or require attribution.
Check
Do you have a way to turn this tool off or remove it if needed? This is basic due diligence. Know how to stop using a tool cleanly, including requesting deletion of your data where the provider permits it.
Check
If your business serves a significant Māori community or holds Māori data, have you considered Māori data sovereignty implications? This is especially relevant for businesses in the Bay of Plenty. Contact Toi Kai Rawa Trust if you need guidance.

Section 5 · Next steps
Three things worth doing this week
You don't need to fix everything at once. These three actions cover the most common gaps for Bay of Plenty small businesses.
Step 5.1
Do the three things that matter most
Action 1 — This week
Update your privacy policy to mention AI. If you have a website privacy policy, add one sentence acknowledging that you may use AI tools to assist with business operations and that customer data is handled in accordance with the Privacy Act 2020. This is a five-minute update that covers a real gap.
Action 2 — This week
Stop pasting real customer data into free AI tools. Use placeholders or descriptions instead of actual names, contact details, or identifying information. If you need to work with real data at scale, look at business-tier accounts with data processing agreements.
Action 3 — This month
Make a simple list of every AI tool your business uses. Include what it does, what data it touches, and a link to its terms of service. It takes 20 minutes and gives you a foundation for any future review.
