Part 4
The Honest Questions
These are for a parent and teen conversation — ideally not right after filling in the radar. Let it breathe first.
1
When you're feeling sad, anxious, or lonely — what do you reach for first? A person or a device?
No right answer. Just honest observation.
2
Is there a relationship on your list that you know needs more attention than you're giving it?
Name the person. Name what's getting in the way.
3
What would it feel like to go 24 hours without any AI conversation or parasocial content?
What does your reaction to that question tell you?
4
Who in your life actually knows you — the real you, including the hard parts?
AI can be optimised to make you feel good. Real friends are the ones who stay when it costs them something.

A word for parents navigating this
The fact that your teenager has relationships with AI chatbots and parasocial creators is not a moral failure — it's the world they've grown up in. The question isn't whether to feel guilty about it. The question is what to do now.

Tristan Harris is right that the technology has moved from capturing attention to capturing attachment. That's a significant shift. But it doesn't mean real relationships are gone — it means they need to be protected and invested in with more intentionality than previous generations had to think about.

The Christian vision of friendship — people who know you, who challenge you, who stay when it costs them something — is not only countercultural right now. It's one of the most profound things the church has to offer a generation that's settling for something far less.
