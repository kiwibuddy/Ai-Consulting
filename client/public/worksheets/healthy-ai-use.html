"AI is like a hammer in the toolbox, not a doctrine in the pulpit."
Kenny Jahng — Barna Group, "AI and the Church: How Pastors Can Lead with Wisdom in a Digital Age" (2025)

The problem isn't that Christians are using AI. Christians are already using it more than non-Christians for work — that's Barna's finding, not an assumption. The problem is that most people have no framework for knowing what kind of question belongs where. This guide gives you that framework in five categories. It ends with a prompt guide you can print and actually keep.

The five-category framework
Know what to ask, and who to ask it to
Category 1
AI is excellent for this
Use it freely and confidently
Use freely
Examples
Drafting emails & letters Research & summarising articles Planning & scheduling Sermon/talk outlines Learning new concepts Translating & editing Creating templates Brainstorming ideas Coding & technical tasks Historical background research
Why it works here
These tasks are informational, functional, and verifiable. The quality of the output can be checked. They don't require wisdom, relationship, or spiritual discernment — just capable execution. AI is genuinely faster and often better at these tasks than working alone. Use it, and use it well.
Category 2
AI is useful but limited here
Use with caution — always verify, always think
Use carefully
Examples
Bible commentary & context Theological background research Sermon illustration ideas Understanding different viewpoints Devotional writing prompts Initial Bible study notes Exploring ethical questions (starting point only) Summarising a theology book
Why to be careful here
AI can hallucinate citations, misrepresent theological positions, and present a confident-sounding answer that is subtly wrong. The Revolution of Ordinaries team warns: "AI also likes to hallucinate — it can make things up out of nowhere." Use AI as a starting point, not a final authority. Always verify against actual sources.
The higher trust Christians have in AI for faith questions (29% vs 23% of non-Christians) is a specific concern flagged by Barna — familiarity can lower appropriate scepticism. Always pray over theological content before using it.
Category 3
Ask a pastor instead
This requires wisdom, relationship, and accountability
Go to your pastor
Examples
Am I reading this Scripture correctly for my situation? Is this decision in line with Scripture? I'm struggling with this theological question personally I need spiritual counsel on a major decision Something in my church is troubling me I want to explore a calling or ministry direction I need accountability around something specific
Why a pastor, not AI
These questions require someone who knows you, knows your context, and is accountable for what they say. AI cannot provide that. David Scotford's research found that "outsourcing spiritual responsibilities to AI risks replacing church leaders with artificial systems." Your pastor is not a search engine — and you shouldn't treat AI like a pastor.
Ray Miller warns that "the interactivity AI brings will feel more like a personal deity that seems all-knowing." That feeling is precisely why these questions belong with a real person who can be wrong, who knows they can be wrong, and who bears responsibility for what they say.
Category 4
Ask a friend instead
This requires genuine relationship, not information
Call a person
Examples
I'm feeling lonely or discouraged today I need someone to listen to what I'm going through I'm not sure what to do about this relationship I need honest feedback about a decision I'm making I want to celebrate something with someone I need prayer — specifically, with someone present Something happened and I just need to talk
Why a friend, not AI
Jack Dodge writes: "Real, authentic relationships cannot be formed in a healthy way with AI — it is not an actual human soul." Comer's research found that 54% of Americans have no intimate friendships. AI makes the loneliness crisis worse, not better — because it simulates connection without providing it. Real friendship requires cost, risk, and mutual vulnerability. AI offers none of those things.
Category 5
Sit with it in prayer instead
This is a God-sized question — not a Google-sized one
Pray first
Examples
What is God calling me to do next? Should I take this opportunity or not? How does God see me right now? What does God want to say to me in this season? Am I where I'm supposed to be? I'm struggling to forgive — what should I do? What is the Spirit prompting in me? What does God think of this situation?
Why prayer, not AI
"Artificial intelligence cannot replace revelation or generate truth from God." — Elder Gong. These questions are not answered by more information — they are answered by encounter with a Person. Dallas Willard said the disciplines cultivate "openness, willingness, and dependence on God." AI cannot make you dependent on God. Only choosing to go to God first does that.
The question to ask yourself: "Is this a question with an answer, or a question that wants to bring me into relationship?" If it's the second, stop. Put the phone down. And pray.
Quick decision: which category is this?
Run through these three questions before you open ChatGPT
1
Could this be checked against verifiable facts? If yes → Category 1 or 2. Use AI, then verify.
2
Does this require someone who knows me and is accountable for their answer? If yes → Category 3 or 4. Call a person.
3
Is what I'm really looking for encounter, not information? If yes → Category 5. Go to God. The answer isn't in a search box.

The test: "Is this question looking for an answer — or is it looking to bring me into relationship?" Information questions → AI may help. Relationship questions → AI cannot help. Wisdom, counsel, friendship, prayer, and encounter are irreducibly human and divine. Use AI for what it's for, and reserve the rest for who it's for.

A word in closing
The goal of this guide is not to make you suspicious of a tool that can genuinely serve you. AI is useful. For the right things, it is very useful. The fact that Christians are already using it more than non-Christians for work tells you that the early movers in the church are not the fearful or the naïve — they're the pragmatic and the curious, which is what you'd hope.

But the second Barna finding matters too: only 8% of Christians want to use AI for spiritual questions. That instinct is right. Not because AI is evil, but because the questions that matter most — who am I, what am I called to, how does God see me, what should I do — are not information problems. They are encounter problems. And encounter requires a living relationship, not a language model.

Use the hammer. Just don't preach with it.
