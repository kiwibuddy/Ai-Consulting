The VERIFY Method
AI Hallucination Detective
AI can be brilliantly useful — and it can also make things up. Completely. Confidently. Without knowing it's wrong. This worksheet teaches you a six-step method for checking any AI claim before you use it, share it, or act on it.

AI tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, and Copilot are genuinely useful. But they hallucinate — that's the technical word for when an AI states something completely wrong with total confidence. The dangerous part is it doesn't know it's wrong. It's not lying. It's just generating text that sounds plausible.

Below are three AI-generated "facts." Your job is to run each through the VERIFY method — six steps that teach you to actually check a claim, not just accept it because it sounds right. Work through each step in order. Don't skip.

⚠️
The one rule before you start
Never use the AI itself to fact-check the AI. That's like asking someone if they were lying and trusting their answer. Use Wikipedia as a starting point, then trace it back to an original source — a news site, government database, museum, or university publication.

Step 2 · Examine the source

R

Search: "Great Wall of China visible from space" and "Great Wall history construction." What do reliable sources say about each claim?

Claim to investigate · History & Culture · New Zealand context
The Treaty of Waitangi
→ AI response when asked about the Treaty of Waitangi:
"The Treaty of Waitangi was signed in 1840 between the British Crown and all Māori tribes in New Zealand. It was written in two languages — English and te reo Māori — and both versions say exactly the same thing. The Treaty gave the Crown full sovereignty over New Zealand, while Māori kept ownership of their land forever. It is now largely considered settled and uncontroversial."
V
Step 1 · Verify the specific claims
This one is the most complex — some parts are partly true, some are wrong, one is a major oversimplification. Separate each claim carefully.
Look closely at: who signed it, whether the two versions say the same thing, what each says about sovereignty, and whether it is "settled."
E
Step 2 · Examine the source
For a country's founding treaty, what sources would be most authoritative — and why might AI struggle with this one specifically?
NZ-specific sources: Te Ara Encyclopedia of New Zealand, NZ government history archives, Waitangi Tribunal records, Te Papa Tongarewa website.

Step 3 · Research independently
The key question: do the English and Māori versions of the Treaty say the same thing? Look this up specifically.
Search: "Treaty of Waitangi English vs Māori version differences" — this is one of the most important debates in New Zealand's history.

Teacher / Facilitator Notes — Answer Guide
Claim 1 — Great Wall: "Visible from space" is a well-documented myth; Chinese astronaut Yang Liwei confirmed he could not see it. Wall construction began Qin Dynasty (~221 BCE), not Ming Dynasty. Length of 21,196 km is a measured figure but varies by methodology. Low-stakes error — useful warm-up.
Claim 2 — Lightning: Global deaths average ~2,000/year (WHO figures), not 6,000. Survival rate is approximately 90% — the "always lethal" claim is false. Lightning does not always strike the tallest object. Lying flat on the ground is outdated and potentially dangerous guidance — the correct advice is crouch low on balls of feet, minimising contact. Safety-critical error — key teachable moment about real-world consequences.
Claim 3 — Treaty of Waitangi: Not all iwi signed; approximately 540 rangatira signed across multiple versions, but some never did. English and Māori versions differ significantly: 'kawanatanga' (governance) ≠ 'sovereignty'; 'tino rangatiratanga' implies chieftainship over taonga. The Treaty is actively contested — the Waitangi Tribunal was established 1975 specifically to hear Māori grievances. Calling it "settled" is itself a political position. Bias error — AI centres colonial English-language perspective. Richest discussion of the three.
A word to close
AI is not the enemy of good thinking. Lazy thinking is. The VERIFY method isn't about being suspicious of every tool — it's about being the kind of thinker who knows the difference between what sounds right and what actually is right. That skill matters more now, not less, because AI makes plausible-sounding wrong answers cheaper and faster to produce than ever before.

The most important thing you did in this worksheet wasn't find the errors. It was practise the habit of looking.
