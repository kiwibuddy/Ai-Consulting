"AI Shame" — and how to navigate your workplace honestly
48.8%
of employees hide their AI use at work to avoid judgement — "AI shame" — even when the work they produce is genuinely better. This is most pronounced at the C-suite level (53.4%), where executives are the most frequent AI users and the most secretive about it. 62.6% of Gen Z workers have submitted AI-assisted work while presenting it as entirely their own.
WalkMe AI in the Workplace Survey 2025 / Fortune, August 2025

This isn't a moral problem — it's a culture problem. Most workplaces haven't defined what transparent, ethical AI use looks like, which means workers are navigating it alone. The questions below help you think clearly about your specific context rather than applying a generic rule.

A note on integrity
For a Christian, the framework for transparency isn't just "what does my workplace culture allow?" It's deeper: who am I when nobody's checking? That's not a rebuke — it's actually liberating. You don't have to decode your organisation's unwritten rules about AI. You can start from a place of character rather than calculation. There's a meaningful difference between not broadcasting every AI-assisted task and actively misrepresenting your work. A person of integrity knows the difference — and stays clearly on the right side of it, regardless of what everyone else is doing.
A
What is your organisation's actual position on AI use — written or unwritten?
Not what it says in a policy document, if one exists. What the real culture is, based on what you observe.
B
Are there tasks in your role where AI use would be genuinely problematic — not just uncomfortable?
Client confidentiality, data privacy, professional accountability, regulated outputs. Be honest — these are real constraints, not excuses.
C
What is your personal standard for transparency about AI use?
There's a meaningful difference between not announcing every AI-assisted task and actively misrepresenting your work. Where is your line?
Your visibility strategy — choose your approach
If your workplace is openly AI-friendly
Make your AI use a visible advantage
Being the person who uses AI well — and can show others how — is a genuine career differentiator. The 58% skills gap in most industries means AI-fluent workers stand out fast.
If your workplace is cautious or unclear
Navigate it quietly — but with integrity
You can use AI for tasks where the output is what matters, without broadcasting it. The key is knowing your personal line and staying clearly on the right side of it.

A closing word
Most AI training tells you how to use the tools. Almost none of it tells you what to do with what the tools give you. That gap — between saving time and deploying it deliberately — is where the real career differentiation happens.

The 52 minutes is not a productivity win. It's a decision point. You can let your employer absorb it through higher output expectations. You can spend it on things that don't compound. Or you can make a deliberate, ongoing investment in the work that makes you genuinely harder to replace. That choice doesn't happen by default. It happens here — in writing, with a date, and someone who knows about it.

And for those of us who are followers of Jesus: the Psalmist asked God to help us number our days wisely. This is one of those days. What you do with this margin is an act of stewardship — not just of time, but of the person God is forming you to be.
