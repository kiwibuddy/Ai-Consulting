The fear of job loss from AI is real. But the more interesting question — the one this worksheet addresses — is not "will AI take my job?" It's "what do the people around me actually value that I provide, and how much of that can an AI do?" Most people have never made this explicit. This worksheet does.

A Christian foundation for this exercise
"Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart, as working for the Lord, not for human masters... It is the Lord Christ you are serving."
Colossians 3:23–24
Notice the scope: "whatever you do." Not "whatever ministry activities you do." This means the specific, ordinary, unremarkable things that make up your actual working week — the judgment calls, the difficult conversations, the institutional memory you carry, the care you bring to a colleague who's struggling. For a Christian, the question "what will they still pay me for?" runs deeper than market value. It connects to calling. The things that are most distinctly and irreducibly you at work may be exactly what you're meant to be doing — not just what you happen to be good at.
The finding that reframes what job security actually means
Stanford researchers found that the workers most at risk are those doing tasks that AI automates. The workers who are growing are those where AI augments human judgement, relationships, and expertise. Job security in 2026 is not about your job title — it's about whether the specific things you do in that role require you to be human.
Erik Brynjolfsson et al., "Canaries in the Coal Mine," Stanford Digital Economy Lab, August 2025

There is a gap between what your job description says and what the people around you actually depend on you for. That gap is where your real career security lives. Most people have never mapped it — because nobody asks them to. This worksheet does three things: it shows you the gap, it maps the specific relationships where your value is embedded, and it helps you write a Human Value Proposition — a clear statement of what makes you irreplaceable that you could actually say out loud to your manager or a future employer.

Part 5 — The visibility problem
Value that isn't visible doesn't protect you
The most dangerous situation in an AI-disrupted workplace is being genuinely valuable but invisible. The people who get protected are the people whose value is legible to decision-makers — before a restructure happens, not after.
The manager conversation
Your manager should be able to articulate your Human Value Proposition before any restructure conversation. That means you need to have made it explicit in your regular 1:1s — not in a crisis.

A closing word
The workplace anxiety most people feel about AI is real — but it's often pointed at the wrong thing. The threat isn't that AI will replace you. The threat is that your real value stays invisible until it's too late to make it legible.

AI can retrieve information, draft documents, and identify patterns. It cannot be shaped by the relationships in your organisation. It cannot carry the institutional memory of why a particular client is difficult, or know that a team member needs support this week, or own a mistake in the way that rebuilds trust. Those things are yours. They are not small. And the people who do them consciously — who make that value visible and irreplaceable — are the ones who will still be employed and thriving when the next wave of automation arrives.

For those of us who follow Jesus: the Colossians instruction wasn't written for heroes doing spectacular things. It was written for ordinary workers in ordinary places. "Whatever you do" includes your spreadsheets, your difficult conversations, your Tuesday afternoon. Work it with all your heart — because that's the person God made you, and no model trained on data has ever been made in anyone's image.
