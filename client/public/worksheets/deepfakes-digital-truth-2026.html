A foundation for discernment

We are called to be salt and light in the world — and that includes the digital world. Technology is changing how fast ideas travel, how easily images can be faked, and how simple it has become to deceive millions of people at once. As Christians we are called to steward our communications — to be people who help shape their communities through what we read, what we believe, and what we choose to pass on. The content we share is not neutral. It builds the world or erodes it. Before you work through this guide, take a moment with what these passages say about how we engage with information in an age when truth is genuinely hard to find.

Vishal Mangalwadi
"God spoke and created the universe. Man speaks and creates culture, which shapes the universe."
Acts 17:11 NIV
"Now the Berean Jews received the message with great eagerness and examined the Scriptures every day to see if what Paul said was true."
The Bereans didn’t passively receive what they were told — they checked. Every day. That same habit applies to everything we encounter now: news articles, viral photos, shocking videos, voice messages, and posts shared by people we trust. The question is not whether to check. It is whether we have built the habit.
Philippians 4:8 NIV
"Whatever is true, whatever is noble, whatever is right... think about such things."
This is a warning against letting outrage, shock, and manufactured fear run your thinking. AI-generated content and misleading media are specifically designed to hijack attention through strong emotion. The counter-habit: actively seek what is actually true rather than being swept along by what feels urgent or dramatic. Don’t let fake news and shock-factor media pull you into rabbit trails away from what is real.
1 Thessalonians 5:21 NIV
"Test everything. Hold on to what is good."
Paul’s command is universal — not just test things you’re suspicious of, or when you have time. Everything. The moment we stop testing is the moment we become useful to those who profit from spreading false content. Holding on to what is good means not passing on what you cannot verify.
Hebrews 5:14 NIV
"Solid food is for the mature, who by constant use have trained themselves to distinguish good from evil."
Discernment is not a gift that arrives fully formed — it is a muscle. The word here is trained, the same word used for athletic practice. Digital discernment works the same way: the more consistently you apply these habits, the faster and more naturally you will sense when something is wrong.
John 8:32 NIV
"You will know the truth, and the truth will set you free."
Freedom here is not only spiritual. When you cannot distinguish what is real online, you are manipulable — your fears, beliefs, and decisions can be shaped by whoever controls the content. Knowing how to find truth is a form of genuine freedom. This guide is a small part of that.
Darrow L. Miller — Why Words?
"Whoever controls language controls history and shapes the future of a people and a nation."
AI-generated images, fabricated voices, and synthetic text are not just individual deceptions. They are a slow rewriting of the language through which a culture understands what is real. You must change the language of a society before you can change the society — which is why defending truth is not a niche concern, but a foundational one. Christians who refuse to spread false content, who check before they share, and who train themselves in discernment are not being cautious. They are stewarding the vocabulary of truth in a moment when it is under serious, sustained attack.
Darrow L. Miller, Discipling Nations: The Power of Truth to Transform Culture, YWAM Publishing · disciplenations.org

Section 1 · AI-Generated Images
Spotting fake photos on social media, news & messaging apps
AI can now generate photorealistic images of events that never happened, people who don't exist, and places that look completely real.
1
Zoom in on faces, hands, and edges. AI still struggles with teeth (too perfect or too many), earrings that don't match, hair along the hairline, and background text that becomes garbled on close inspection. Zoom to 100% and scan these areas.
2
Check the physics. Shadows that don't match the light source, reflections in eyes or glasses that show impossible scenes, and skin texture that is unnaturally smooth or oddly uniform are all AI tells. Real photos have natural imperfection.
3
Do a reverse image search. Right-click (desktop) or press-hold (mobile) and "Search image with Google." If the same photo appears with different captions or a different original context, it may be manipulated or mislabelled.
4
Check metadata (for photos shared as files). Right-click the file → Properties (PC) or Get Info (Mac). AI-generated images often show unusual creation times, editing software, or missing camera data that real photos carry.
5
Run it through a free AI detection tool like TrueMedia.org or Hive Moderation. These are not foolproof, but flagging is a useful additional signal — especially when combined with your own visual check.
⚠ Red flags at a glance
•
Perfect, uniform skin with no pores, wrinkles, or texture variation
•
Blurred or warped backgrounds, especially near hair or clothing edges
•
Accessories or jewellery that differ between sides of the face
•
Text in signs, logos, or backgrounds that is garbled or nonsensical
•
The image makes you feel shocked or outraged — pause before sharing

Section 2 · AI-Generated Video (Deepfakes)
Spotting fake video on YouTube, social media & TV
Deepfake videos can put words in real people's mouths, show events that never happened, and make trusted figures appear to say things they never said.
1
Watch the eyes. Real humans blink every 2–10 seconds spontaneously. AI-generated faces often stare without blinking for unnaturally long periods, and when they do blink, it looks mechanical — lacking the subtle muscle movements around the eyes that accompany genuine blinks.
2
Watch the head movements. Most deepfake models train primarily on front-facing data. When a synthetic face rotates to a full profile, the rendering often breaks down — look for blurring, distortion, or the face becoming unnatural at angles.
3
Check lip sync. Does the mouth movement exactly match every word? In deepfake videos there are often slight mismatches, especially on consonants. The jaw and cheek muscles also move unnaturally in synthetic faces.
4
Look at lighting and shadows. Look for unnatural lighting, overly smooth skin textures, flickering edges, or blurred backgrounds. Does the light on the face match the direction of light sources in the scene? AI frequently gets this wrong.
5
Verify the source independently. Search for the same clip or event on established news outlets. If major outlets aren't reporting it and the video is going viral on social media only, treat it with strong scepticism.
6
Check for C2PA content credentials. Some platforms are beginning to show "Content Credentials" badges on verified authentic content. Their absence doesn't prove a fake, but their presence indicates the content has a verified chain of origin.
⚠ Red flags at a glance
•
Flickering or shimmering around the face, especially at hair and chin edges
•
The face looks slightly "pasted on" or disconnected from body movement
•
Micro-expressions are absent — the face is too still between words
•
Teeth disappear or blur when the mouth opens
•
A public figure says something wildly out of character — verify first

Section 3 · AI Voice Cloning
When the voice sounds exactly like someone you know
AI can clone a person's voice from as little as 3–30 seconds of audio pulled from social media, voicemail, or video. The result can fool family members, friends, and colleagues.
1
Listen for unnatural speech rhythm. Cloned voices often have an overly smooth, steady pace, strange rhythm, or a lack of natural breathing sounds. The emotional depth may feel flat, hollow, or overly dramatic — too perfect in some ways, subtly wrong in others.
2
Pause before reacting. These calls are designed to elicit an intense emotional reaction. If you do get an emergency call from a loved one, pause for a minute to gather your thoughts and respond instead of reacting. Scammers deliberately trigger the fear response.
3
Ask a question only they would know. A family member's middle name, the street you grew up on, a shared memory, the name of a childhood pet. AI cannot answer questions it wasn't given data for — and a scammer won't know either.
4
Hang up and call back on their real number. Don't trust the voice. Call the person who supposedly contacted you and verify the story. Use a phone number you know is theirs. Do not call back the number that rang you — it may be spoofed.
5
Never send money through untraceable methods. Wire transfers, gift cards, and cryptocurrency are irreversible and impossible to recover. No legitimate emergency requires gift card payments. This is a universal scam signal.
⚠ Red flags at a glance
•
Extreme urgency — "you need to act NOW before it gets worse"
•
Request for payment via gift cards, wire transfer, or crypto
•
Caller says "don't tell anyone" or "the police can't know"
•
Slightly odd tone, rhythm, or an echo in the voice
•
The story involves a foreign country, arrest, or accident — classic patterns
✓ The single most effective protection
★
Establish a family safe word now — a unique word or phrase only your family knows. If anyone ever calls claiming an emergency, ask for the safe word. If they don't have it, hang up and call back on the real number.

Section 4 · AI-Generated Text
Spotting fake news articles, social posts & AI-written content
AI can generate convincing news articles, social media posts, and opinion pieces at scale. Knowing the linguistic patterns helps you slow down before sharing.
Signs in the writing itself
1
Unnaturally smooth, uniform tone. AI text rarely has a genuine point of view. It hedges everything, presents "both sides" even when unnecessary, and avoids the specific personal voice real writers have.
2
Corporate transition words. Phrases like "it's important to note that," "in today's world," "it goes without saying," "delve into," and "testament to" appear at high rates in AI writing. Look for these clustering together.
3
Perfect grammar, no personality. Real human writing has quirks, specific references, personal voice, and natural variation. AI-generated text tends to be grammatically perfect but oddly generic.
4
Vague or unverifiable citations. AI often invents quotes, statistics, and sources that sound real but don't exist. Check any quote or statistic that seems compelling before you repeat it.
Verify the source and story
5
Check the author. Does the byline belong to a real person? A quick search often reveals whether an "author" has any real web presence or whether the profile was created recently with no history.
6
Is it covered elsewhere? A real news story will be reported by multiple independent outlets. If a shocking claim only appears on one unfamiliar site, it may be fabricated.
7
Check the URL carefully. Fake news sites often use URLs like "ABCnews.com.co" or "CNN-breaking.net" — designed to look legitimate at a glance but slightly wrong on inspection.
8
Use a fact-checking site. Snopes.com, FactCheck.org, AFP Fact Check, and Reuters Fact Check all maintain databases of debunked stories. Search the headline before sharing.

Section 5 · Fake Profiles & Friendship Fraud
When the "person" you're talking to online doesn't exist
AI-generated profile photos and AI-assisted conversations allow scammers to maintain convincing fake identities for weeks or months — especially targeting lonely or isolated people.

The Guardian reported in March 2026 that friendship fraud targeting older people is rising sharply — where scammers spend weeks building a relationship before asking for money. The AI profile photo is often the first layer of the deception.

1
Reverse image search the profile photo. AI-generated faces don't exist anywhere else online. If the profile picture appears on multiple unrelated sites or doesn't appear at all, both are warning signs.
2
Check the account history. When was it created? How many posts? Does it have real friends or followers with genuine history? A new account with few connections but fast engagement is suspicious.
3
Be alert to "romance trajectory" patterns. Too much attention too fast, escalating intimacy, beautiful profile with apparently glamorous life, never able to meet in person, always has a reason a video call won't work.
4
Watch for the financial request pattern. Friendship or romance fraud almost always builds toward a financial request — an emergency, a stranded situation, a business investment "opportunity," medical costs.
5
Tell someone you trust. Scammers work to isolate victims and make them feel the relationship is special and private. If you feel uncomfortable telling a trusted friend or family member about an online relationship, that discomfort is important information.
⚠ Red flags at a glance
•
Intensely romantic or flattering tone within days of first contact
•
Claims to be overseas, military, working on a ship, or in a remote location
•
Has an implausibly perfect appearance but can never do a live video call
•
Discourages you from checking their identity or talking to others about them
•
Any request for money, gift cards, or cryptocurrency — even small amounts to start

Section 6 · Emergency Voice Scams — URGENT READ
When you hear your family member's voice saying they're in danger
This is the most psychologically powerful scam technique AI has enabled. The voice sounds exactly like someone you love. They say they need money immediately. Your brain triggers a panic response.
⚠ If you receive this call RIGHT NOW — follow these steps
Stop. Breathe. Do not send money yet.
Scammers exploit panic. The goal is to make you act before you think. Your brain's fear response is their primary weapon. Knowing this in advance is your best protection.
1
Tell the caller you need to call them back. Any legitimate emergency allows time for a callback. A scammer will pressure you not to hang up.
2
Hang up and call the person directly on their real saved number — not by calling the number that rang you. If they answer, it was a scam. If no answer, call another family member to verify.
3
Ask the personal question only they would know. Before you do anything, ask for your family's safe word, or ask something AI can't answer — their first pet's name, your grandmother's maiden name.
4
Never send money via gift cards, wire transfer, or crypto. These are irreversible. No legitimate emergency requires gift cards. Police, hospitals, and lawyers do not ask for payment this way.
5
Report it. In NZ: Netsafe (netsafe.org.nz). In Australia: Scamwatch (scamwatch.gov.au). In the US: FTC (reportfraud.ftc.gov). Reporting helps protect others.
Set up your family safe word — do it now, before you ever need it
Choose a word or short phrase that is unusual, memorable, and that only your immediate family knows. If anyone ever calls claiming an emergency, ask for this word first. If they don't have it — it's a scam.

⚠ Classic emergency scam patterns — recognise them
•
"I've been arrested and need bail money — please don't tell mum/dad yet"
•
"I was in a car accident in [foreign country] and need money for the hospital"
•
"I'm stranded and my wallet was stolen — please send me something on [app]"
•

•

A closing word — from the Communication Sphere
The goal of this guide is not to make you afraid of everything you see online. Most of what you encounter is real. The goal is what Paul describes in 1 Thessalonians — to test what you see and hold onto what is good. That takes a moment of pause. A second look. A willingness to check before you share.

Christians who do this consistently are not being paranoid. They're being responsible stewards of their voice and their influence. When you stop sharing false content, you reduce the noise that harms real people. When you protect your family from voice scams, you are loving your neighbour with practical wisdom. And when you approach all of this with the gentleness of 1 Peter — not with judgement toward those who were fooled, but with calm equipping — you embody exactly what the Communication Sphere is meant to be.
