John Mark Comer writes: "Any serious effort toward spiritual formation starts not with effort — it starts with rest." Relevant Magazine, 2024 That's a strange place to begin a four-week challenge. But it's the right one. The goal of this guide is not to make you more disciplined. It's to help you discover what you actually want when the noise settles — and whether that aligns with who you say you are in Christ.

Scholars at Fractal: The Attention War note that algorithms are designed on intermittent variable rewards — the same mechanism that drives slot machine addiction. Fractal: The Attention War, 2024 Every scroll, every notification, every refresh is a small pull of that lever. You are not weak for finding this hard. The system is engineered to make it hard. What follows is a counter-formation practice, one week at a time.

1
Week 1 · The Honest Question
Beginning where you actually are
"What am I avoiding by staying online?"
This week's practice
30-minute daily offline window — no phone, no screens, no podcast. Just you and whatever emerges.
Most scrolling isn't really about what's on the screen. It's about what's under it. Loneliness, anxiety, boredom, grief, a conversation we're dreading — the online world provides an infinite supply of things to look at instead. The Fractal research team calls this "algorithmic collapse": we outsource our emotional regulation to feeds that have no interest in our health, only in our engagement. Fractal: The Attention War, 2024 This week's practice isn't about willpower. It's about curiosity. What actually comes up when you stop filling the space?
"
"Why, my soul, are you downcast? Why so disturbed within me? Put your hope in God, for I will yet praise him, my Saviour and my God."
Psalm 42:11 · The Psalmist names what's underneath — he doesn't scroll past it
How to do the 30-minute offline window
1.
Choose the same time each day — morning works well before the day has momentum. Put your phone in another room. Not face-down. Another room.
2.
Don't fill it with something else. No podcast, no music, no book (yet). Sit. Walk outside. Make tea. Let the quiet be loud.
3.
When something uncomfortable surfaces — a feeling, a worry, a question — write it down instead of reaching for your phone. That's the whole practice.

2
Week 2 · Deepening
Keep Week 1's practice and add this
"What does silence feel like?"
This week's practice
One full offline morning per week — from waking until noon. Phone off. Nothing digital.
Dallas Willard named silence among the core spiritual disciplines — alongside prayer, fasting, and Scripture. Dallas Willard, The Spirit of the Disciplines It's on that list for a reason: silence is where we discover what's actually there when the external noise stops. John Mark Comer writes that the "spirit of Joy and the spirit of Hurry cannot live in the same house." John Mark Comer, Practicing the Way archives Most of us have not chosen hurry. We've simply never found out what happens when we stop.
"
"The Lord is in his holy temple; let all the earth be silent before him."
Habakkuk 2:20 · Silence is not emptiness — it is orientation toward presence
How to do your offline morning
1.
Choose one morning — Saturday works for many. Don't turn your phone on until noon. Tell someone the night before so no one worries.
2.
Do whatever you want — walk, cook, pray, read a physical book, sit in your garden. The rule is simple: nothing digital.
3.
Notice the urge to check. Don't fight it — just observe it. Where does the urge live in your body? What feeling is it covering?
4.
Pray at some point during the morning. Not with a podcast. Not with notes. Just talk to God, and then stop talking and listen.

3
Week 3 · Conviction
Keep Weeks 1 & 2 and add this
"What do I actually believe when no one is reinforcing it?"
This week's practice
Scripture without commentary, podcast, or AI interpretation — just you and the text, in silence.
Samuel James observes that the internet presents a profoundly liturgical environment that constantly reinforces a particular vision of reality. Samuel James, Digital Liturgies, 2023 That reinforcement shapes what you believe — subtly, below the level of conscious choice. This week's question is a harder one: when you are not surrounded by your tribe, your podcast, your feed, your AI assistant — what do you actually hold to be true? The RSIS research found that many users begin to wonder whether they even know their own preferences anymore. RSIS International, 2025 The same erosion can happen to faith.
"
"Your word is a lamp for my feet, a light on my path."
Psalm 119:105 · The lamp illuminates what's in front of you — not what's trending on someone else's timeline
How to read Scripture without assistance
1.
Choose one short passage — a Psalm, a parable, a chapter of John. Read it slowly. Read it twice. No commentary app. No Bible Project video. No AI summary.
2.
Ask one question: "What is God saying to me in this, today?" Not "what is the correct interpretation." What is God saying to you.
3.
Write down whatever comes. Even if it feels thin. Even if you're not sure. The practice is learning to trust that God speaks to you directly — not only through mediated content.
Daily Scripture reading — your own engagement
What passage did you read? What one thing struck you — not from a podcast or commentary, but from the text itself?
And the harder question: what do you believe right now, in this week, without needing it to be endorsed by anyone?

4
Week 4 · The Full Practice
The destination all three weeks have been moving toward
"What would it look like to hold technology loosely?"
This week's practice
One full Sabbath day — phone off, from waking to sleeping. Not reduced. Off.
Eugene Peterson called the secular day off a "bastard sabbath" — a pale imitation that looks like rest but is really just recuperation for more productivity. Eugene Peterson, cited by John Mark Comer — Practicing the Way archives Biblical Sabbath is structurally different. It is not recuperation. It is defiance. It says: my value is not in my output. My identity is not in my productivity. God made the world and rested — and he invites us into that rest, not as a reward for finishing, but as a practice for beings made in his image. The Practicing the Way team writes: "Sabbath is a practice to fight against the chronic restlessness of our condition." Practicing the Way, practicingthewayarchives.org One day without technology is not the goal. It's the training ground for holding all of it loosely, every day.
"
"Come to me, all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you and learn from me, for I am gentle and humble in heart, and you will find rest for your souls."
Matthew 11:28–29 · Rest as invitation, not reward — Jesus offers it freely, before anything is done
How to do a full Sabbath day — phone off
1.
Prepare the day before. Tell people you'll be offline. Set an out-of-office if needed. There is nothing so urgent it cannot wait 24 hours. (If there genuinely is — that's important data about your life.)
2.
Turn the phone off. Not silent. Not face-down. Off. This is the practice. The anxiety you feel in the first hour is the whole point — observe it without feeding it.
3.
Do things that are genuinely restorative, relational, and unhurried. Worship. Walk. Cook and eat slowly. Pray. Read. Be with people you love. Do nothing. Let the day be long.
4.
At the end of the day — before you turn your phone back on — write down what you noticed. What emerged without the device? What would you like to carry into the week?
A note on the anxiety
The first time you do this, the anxiety may feel disproportionate to the situation. That anxiety is real data. It tells you something about how dependent your nervous system has become on the constant low-grade stimulation of the device. Don't judge it. Just observe it. Then, gently, choose the Sabbath anyway.

The four-week reflection
"What changed — and what do you want to do about it?"
These four weeks were not designed to make you a perfect practitioner of digital abstinence. They were designed to give you enough data about yourself to make intentional choices. What you do with what you've discovered is the real question. Dallas Willard wrote that disciplines are not merely rituals — they are "vital means to transform character." Dallas Willard, The Spirit of the Disciplines The practices only matter if they open you to grace. The Sabbath is not the destination. It is a door, practiced weekly, that keeps opening you toward the God who rested on the seventh day and said it was very good.
