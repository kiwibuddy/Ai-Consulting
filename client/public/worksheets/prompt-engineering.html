Thinking Harder, Not Less
Most students think AI reduces the need to think. It's the opposite. The quality of what AI gives you is a direct reflection of the quality of thinking you put in. This workshop teaches you how to think on paper — before you type a single word.
"AI is a mirror of your thinking quality. Sharp thinkers get sharp outputs. Lazy thinkers get mush."

When you type a vague question into AI, you get a vague answer. This isn't a bug — it's a feature. AI is giving you exactly what you asked for. The problem is that most people ask badly. They type the first thing that comes to mind, skim the response, and assume it's good. It usually isn't.

A well-engineered prompt takes 30 seconds more thought and gets you an answer that's five times more useful. This workshop shows you the difference — and makes you practise it until it becomes automatic.

Part 1 — See the difference
Bad, OK, and Good — side by side
All three prompts are asking about the same topic: the causes of World War One. Look at what changes between them and why it matters.
Quality: Bad
Lazy prompt
"Tell me about World War One."
What's missing: No context about who's asking or why. No role defined. No format specified. No constraints. AI will generate something generic, possibly 800 words you don't need, starting from 1914, covering every country, with no connection to your actual question.
Quality: OK
Getting closer
"What were the main causes of World War One? Give me a list."
Better because: Asks for a specific thing (causes) and a format (list). Still missing: No context — Year 10 student? University scholar? No role. No constraints on length or complexity. You'll get a list, but it may be too shallow or too academic for what you need.
Quality: Good
Sharp prompt
"I'm a Year 10 student studying for a history exam. Act as a patient history teacher. Explain the four main causes of World War One in plain English using MAIN (Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, Nationalism). Give me one clear paragraph per cause, under 80 words each. No jargon."
Why it works: Context (Year 10, exam prep). Role (patient teacher). Format (MAIN framework, one paragraph each). Constraints (under 80 words, plain English, no jargon). The AI now knows exactly what to produce — and it will.
Part 2 — The CREF Framework
Four ingredients every good prompt needs
You don't need all four in every prompt — but if your answer is bad, one of these is usually missing. Adapted from the Wellington prompt engineering framework.
C
Context
Who are you? What's the situation? What's the purpose?
"I'm a Year 11 student writing a persuasive essay on…"
R
Role
Who should the AI act as? What expertise or voice do you need?
"Act as an experienced biology teacher explaining to a beginner…"
E
Examples
Show the AI what a good answer looks like. Give a sample or format.
"Use this structure: Claim — Evidence — Explanation — Link back."
F
Format & Constraints
Length, tone, complexity level, what to include or avoid.
"Under 150 words. Plain English. No bullet points. Year 9 level."

The real lesson
The best prompts look like good thinking written down. That's not a coincidence — that's the point. When you engineer a prompt carefully, you're actually doing the cognitive work that most people skip: defining what you need, clarifying who you're talking to, specifying what you want the output to look like. That process makes you smarter, not more dependent.

AI doesn't replace thinking. It rewards it.
