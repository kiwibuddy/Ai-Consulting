The idea behind this worksheet
"You are what you love — and you love what you habitually do. Liturgies — habits, routines, rituals — are the means by which our hearts are calibrated."
James K.A. Smith — You Are What You Love (2016) · Smith calls habits "calibration technologies" that shape our desires before we think about them
Why habits, not intentions
Most families have good values but default habits. The algorithms are designed to fill every gap with a screen. The only thing that changes a default is replacing it with something else — deliberately, repeatedly, until it becomes the new default.
Why four weeks
Research suggests it takes around 66 days to form a new habit — not 21 as the old myth claimed. Four weeks won't wire it permanently, but it's enough to discover whether a habit is worth keeping. That discovery is the point.

Family Reading Night
Same time, same place, every week — reading together
2
What this looks like
Pick one evening per week where everyone reads — together but not necessarily the same thing. 20–30 minutes. Phones in the basket. You can read aloud together, or each read your own book in the same room. The rhythm of being physically present and mentally engaged without screens is itself formative.

Week 3 · Add to Weeks 1 & 2
The Phone Basket
Phones off, presence on — a daily boundary at the door
3
What this looks like
Put a basket at your front door — or in the kitchen, wherever works. When you arrive home, phones go in the basket. During dinner, phones go in the basket. This isn't about banning devices forever. It's about creating a threshold — a physical moment that signals: here, we are present to each other.

A note on resistance
Your kids will resist this. So will you. That resistance is data — it tells you how deeply habituated the default has become. Name it honestly, but don't negotiate around it. The basket is the rule, not a suggestion.

Week 4 · All four habits active
The Shared Project
Build something together — anything that takes more than one session
4
What this looks like
Choose something that takes at least 3–4 sessions to complete. Cook through a recipe book. Build something from wood. Plant a garden bed. Write a family letter. Start a prayer journal together. Do a puzzle. The purpose is to create a shared endeavour that keeps drawing you back to the same table — not to produce a perfect result.

A word for families who made it through
You are swimming against a very strong current. The entire attention economy is designed to fill your default moments — the commute, the wait, the gap between tasks — with a screen. The fact that you spent four weeks deliberately doing otherwise is genuinely countercultural.

James K.A. Smith is right: you don't become what you believe; you become what you habitually do. The liturgies — the small, repeated practices — are where formation actually happens. Not in the big moments. In the ordinary texture of Tuesday evening.

These four habits won't fix everything. But they create the conditions for something better to grow. And that is exactly what families in the image of God are called to do — tend the space where flourishing becomes possible.
