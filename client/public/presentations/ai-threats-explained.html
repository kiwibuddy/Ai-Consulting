Introduction

Why this paper exists

If a friend asked you over coffee this weekend: "How worried should I be about AI? What should I actually do?": could you answer without slipping into either dismissal or doom?

Most of us can't, and that's not a personal failing. The public conversation about AI swings between "it's just a tool, calm down" and "we're all out of work by 2027" with very little careful thinking in the middle. This paper is an attempt at that middle.

It borrows a structure from the way governments think about other risks — earthquakes, pandemics, infrastructure failures, and applies it to artificial intelligence. The basic idea is simple: not all bad scenarios are equally likely or equally bad, and the right response depends on which one you're facing. So instead of one undifferentiated "AI risk" story, the paper describes five levels.

Level 1 is roughly where we are right now. Level 5 is the civilisational catastrophe scenario that gets most of the headlines. The middle three are the territory where most actual preparation happens.

What you'll find in each level:

  • What it looks and feels like in daily life
  • The specific things that would tell us we've moved up a level
  • What to actually do, in plain language
  • What not to do (which is often more important)
  • The strongest argument that the level is overstated

Throughout, you can click on the to read more — definitions of jargon, sources for statistics, links to further reading. The body of the paper is meant to be read straight through. The modals are there if you want to go deeper on any particular point.

A note on tone: I've tried to write this honestly, which means saying when something is uncertain, when researchers disagree, and when I think the popular narrative is wrong. Where this paper has a point of view, it's that calibrated calm is the right posture: serious enough to act, sober enough not to panic, grounded enough to keep loving the people in front of you.

Level 01 · Where we are now

The Familiar Tide

AI as a powerful productivity tool. Real harms exist, but they're contained. Most of life is recognisably normal.

This is the world you woke up in this morning. AI helps people write emails and code; it occasionally produces a that makes the news; some specific occupations are visibly stressed; institutions are debating regulation. Daily life — your job, your bank, your kids' school, your rubbish collection, is functioning the way it did five years ago, with some new tools layered on top.

The 2026 describes the current moment with admirable precision: AI systems can solve complex problems across many fields, achieve gold-medal performance on the International Mathematical Olympiad, and exceed PhD-level performance on certain science benchmarks. They also still hallucinate, fail in long tasks, and can't replace human judgement in physical or relational work.

What you'll notice in daily life

For most adults: AI tools are now in your workplace, often unevenly. The colleagues who use them well are quietly becoming more productive. The ones who refuse are starting to show up in performance reviews. Junior roles in some fields — copywriting, paralegal, junior analyst, customer service, are visibly compressed. Senior judgement, the kind AI can't substitute for, has become more valuable, not less.

For households with children: AI companions and chatbots are now widespread on phones and tablets. Some kids are forming relationships with them. The long-term psychological effects are an open scientific question; some studies suggest mild harm, some suggest none. .

For older adults: the is real, growing, and devastating. People aged 60 and over lost about US$7.7 billion to fraud in 2025, up roughly 60% from 2024. Voice-cloning is a substantial driver.

What to actually do at this level

The honest list is short and specific. None of it requires you to believe any particular forecast about AI. All of it makes you better off regardless.

For your identity and family: set up on your primary email, your bank, and your government identity portal. Move family chats to . Establish a : a few unrelated words, shared in person, never written digitally — for any phone call asking for money. .

For your finances: standard emergency reserves of 3 to 6 months of expenses. Assemble a basic , physical copies of essential documents in waterproof storage, plus a small cash reserve. Avoid in either direction.

For your career: if your work is in a category visibly compressing, start a . Build skills that combine human judgement, physical or relational presence, and AI fluency. Workers who refuse AI are becoming visible; workers who only do AI-substitutable tasks are exposed.

For your community: meet your neighbours. you could borrow from in a crisis. This isn't prepping; it's how communities have always handled disruption.

For your attention: establish — physical books, longhand, conversation, prayer, walking. The skill of sustained unaided attention is becoming scarce, and therefore valuable. For children especially, this matters more than almost anything else.

For your civic life: — a school board, a church committee, a council submission. Individual voice has highest leverage at this level.

What not to do

The most common mistakes at Level 1 are over-reactions, not under-reactions. Don't liquidate retirement savings or major assets on the basis of AI doom forecasts. Don't relocate to off-grid arrangements that cut you off from your professional and community networks. Don't keep your kids entirely off AI tools, children kept fully off AI in a Level-1 world arrive at adulthood disadvantaged. Don't lecture friends and family about AI risk in ways that frame them as fools; the cost-benefit on relational capital is poor and the persuasion benefit is near zero.

The strongest case that this level overstates the threat

A serious case can be made that AI is following the well-trodden path of every general-purpose technology — electricity, the internet, smartphones — and that current alarm reflects pattern-matching to past anxieties more than novel risk. The Knight First Amendment Institute concluded political misinformation is "not primarily an AI problem." Empirical labour-market studies through mid-2025 found limited or no significant displacement effects from AI adoption to date. Daron Acemoglu's recent work suggests aggregate productivity gains may be modest, perhaps a 0.6% boost to GDP by 2035 in the cautious scenario.

A reasonable observer might conclude we're at Level 0.5, not Level 1. The framework should be willing to hold that possibility honestly. None of the recommended actions become wrong in that scenario; they just become less urgent.

Level 02 · Where we're heading

Pervasive Disruption

AI saturates knowledge work. The information environment becomes openly adversarial. Trust in shared facts becomes harder to maintain.

Level 2 is the world we slide into when AI capabilities continue to compound, regulation lags behind, and the tools cross from "useful for some tasks" to "default for most knowledge work." Daily life is recognisable. Trust is not.

You'll know this transition is happening because you'll feel it in the quality of the information environment. Voice cloning will become so cheap and good that any phone call from an unknown number requesting urgency will be assumed to be a scam. Any video of a politician saying something controversial will be greeted with "is it real?" as the first reaction, not the third. Universities and professional bodies will be in the middle of urgent rewrites of their assessment frameworks because AI-mediated cheating is breaking the existing systems.

A real example, happening right now

In April 2026, two released models capable of finding software security flaws — : by themselves. to about 50 vetted industry partners through a programme called Project Glasswing. Two days later, OpenAI released "GPT-5.4-Cyber," followed by GPT-5.5-Cyber on 30 April, broadly available to thousands of verified defenders.

The UK's AI Safety Institute evaluated both. GPT-5.5 scored 71.4% on Expert-tier cybersecurity tasks. Mythos scored 68.6%. Both are roughly an order of magnitude above the previous generation. Two opposing theories of how to safely deploy this kind of capability are now being tested in production simultaneously: narrow restriction versus verified broad access. Whichever one proves defensible will shape every future capability tier.

This is not a hypothetical Level 2 scenario. It's a real working example of how Level 2 capability disclosure operates. And it sits alongside criminal infrastructure that is already operational: the , documented by Kaspersky in November 2025, is currently used by a criminal group called TA584 to break into corporate networks. It uses Ethereum smart contracts to make its command-and-control infrastructure essentially unkillable.

What this means for ordinary people

For your work: roles compress, they don't all vanish. Firms restructure around what people are calling "humans plus agents" — typically one senior person managing 5 to 10 AI that previously required 5 to 10 junior humans. Mid-career professionals without AI fluency face involuntary career changes. The wage premium for verifiable, high-judgement, high-trust work rises.

For your family: voice-cloning and identity-impersonation scams are routine. Family verification protocols become essential, not paranoid. Children's media consumption is dominated by synthetic content. AI companionship for adolescents and lonely adults becomes a measurable mental-health concern.

For information: default scepticism becomes correct. The "liar's dividend" emerges, bad actors successfully claim genuine evidence is fake, because the public has lost the ability to distinguish. Institutions that invest in become more credible than those that don't.

What to actually do at this level

Everything from Level 1, plus:

Harden everything in the household: hardware keys for everyone over 14. on every laptop. Disable unnecessary location services and advertising IDs on every phone. , the way you clear browser cookies. Audit your social-media voice and video footprint: three seconds is enough for cloning.

Move decisively into hybrid skills if your career is exposed. Cultivate at least one income stream not dependent on a single employer. Build verifiable reputation: publications, repeat clients, named recommendations. Pseudonymous output is rapidly losing value.

Lengthen financial reserves to 6 to 12 months. Avoid concentration in directly-disrupted industries. Maintain ordinary insurance, with identity-theft and cyber-fraud rider where available.

Strengthen mutual-aid relationships with neighbours, congregation, and professional network. Build a local skill-and-resource map: who can fix things, who has medical training, who has land. This is community work, not prepping.

Practice "epistemic literacy": the deliberate habit of pausing to check the source, author, and context of any content before sharing it or acting on it. Maintain a written list of named human sources you trust. Reduce passive social-media intake. Increase deliberate, slow reading.

What not to do

Don't assume the institutions you trust are immune. Banks, schools, and courts are demonstrably vulnerable to deepfake-mediated fraud at this level. Verify even when verification feels rude.

Don't double down on a single employer or single skill. Disruption at Level 2 is fast in narrow places — entire occupational subcategories can compress in 18 months.

Don't let children form their primary relationships with AI companions. Treat it like sugar: fine in moderation, harmful as a primary diet.

Don't become an AI evangelist or an AI doomer in your friend group. Both postures lose you credibility you'll need at higher levels.

The strongest case that Level 2 isn't inevitable

Three substantive arguments push back. First, capability scaling may flatten, the 2026 IAISR notes that recent gains came largely from inference-time techniques rather than raw model scale, and these may have diminishing returns. Second, regulation may bite faster than expected: the EU AI Act, China's Safety Governance Framework 2.0, and US executive actions create real friction. Third, the Forecasting Research Institute's near-term accuracy assessment found that both experts and superforecasters underestimated AI progress on benchmarks but neither group has been reliably accurate on social and economic impact, which is mediated by adoption frictions and slow-moving institutions.

Level 2 is plausible. It is not certain.

Level 03 · A plausible mid-term scenario

Systemic Stress

Multiple sectors strain at once. Institutional trust visibly cracks. Civil-defence preparedness becomes operational, not aspirational.

Level 3 is the territory where the disruption stops being something that happens to specific industries and becomes something that affects the basic functioning of daily life across a whole society. It's not an apocalyptic scenario. It's a hard, sustained, multi-year period of stress.

What would make this level identifiable: aggregate unemployment in knowledge sectors rises and stays elevated for two or more quarters across multiple sectors at once, not just compression in specific roles. A frontier AI lab triggers an "ASL-4" capability threshold (the highest current safety tier, reserved for systems that could meaningfully help build weapons or fully automate AI research). A major democratic election in a developed country is publicly contested specifically because of AI media disputes that the courts struggle to resolve cleanly. AI agents are involved in critical-infrastructure incidents — financial, medical, transport, energy, with significant downstream impact lasting more than a week.

This isn't where we are. It might not be where we go. But it's the level the framework has to take seriously, because if any of those indicators arrives, the response window narrows fast.

What this would mean for ordinary people

For your work: even mid-career professionals with strong skills experience involuntary career changes. Retraining systems and social safety nets come under real pressure. The gap between AI-fluent and AI-resistant workers becomes a primary driver of inequality, alongside existing factors.

For your community: institutional trust visibly cracks. Some courts, banks, and regulatory bodies are weakened by the cumulative effect of repeated deepfake-mediated incidents and the political backlash to them. Civil-defence agencies move from "useful background reference" to "you should know who they are and how they communicate."

For your information environment: synthetic content is the default, not the exception. Verifying the origin of any media before acting on it becomes a routine cognitive habit, like checking a website is HTTPS before entering a password. Maintaining a small list of named, accountable human sources you trust becomes essential, not optional.

Through all of this: most things still work. Power, water, food, basic services. The who think Level 3 is reachable still mostly think that even at this level, the country runs.

What to actually do

Everything from Levels 1 and 2, plus:

Civil-defence preparation becomes operational. Build out the your government already recommends. Two distinct kits, not one: a . Add basic shelter-in-place capability: N95 masks, plastic sheeting, duct tape — as standard preparedness, not AI-specific paranoia.

Activate mutual-aid networks. Congregational, neighbourhood, professional. The community-led centre model that NZ Civil Defence already plans around, local marae, halls, churches: is exactly the right pattern. Connect to your local Civil Defence group ahead of need, not during a crisis.

Prioritise paid-off shelter where possible. The single highest-value durable resilience asset most households can hold is a paid-off (or substantially paid-down) home. Reserves to 12+ months. Diversified across institutions.

Information discipline becomes cognitive habit. Treat unsourced news as hostile until proven otherwise. Government emergency channels — your country's Civil Defence agency, public broadcaster, become primary trusted sources during incidents. Teach children explicit verification habits.

Contemplative practice moves from "good for you" to "operationally necessary." — the disciplines that hold under stress are the same ones that have always held under stress. Communities that have weathered sustained civilisational stress historically — early Christian networks under Roman persecution, monastic orders during the fall of Rome, mutual-aid networks during the Great Depression, the Confessing Church under National Socialism — share the same operational pattern: thick local relationships, embodied liturgical practice, intergenerational depth.

Distribute authority. Pastors, executives, school heads who centralise information and decision-making in themselves create organisations that fail when they fail. At Level 3, the probability of any individual leader being incapacitated or unavailable rises substantially. Identify five people who could lead your work in your absence. Train them now.

What not to do

Don't isolate. The single largest historical predictor of poor outcomes during sustained stress is loss of social network. Even introverts need 3 to 5 reliable in-person relationships.

Don't panic-relocate. Moving to a "safer" rural area without existing relationships is statistically worse than staying in a stressed urban area where you have ties.

Don't move to speculative assets in panic. The empirical record on selling during sustained crises is uniformly negative.

Don't become a single source of truth for your community. Distribute, train, raise up.

The strongest case against this level

History shows that societies absorb new general-purpose technologies over multi-decade periods, with painful but bounded transitions, not sudden cross-sector collapses. Tony Blair Institute scenarios put substantial AI labour-market impact closer to 2035 than 2030. Acemoglu's empirical position is that genuinely transformative AI requires complementary investment in workflows, infrastructure, and human capital that takes a generation. Level 3 may be a 2035 to 2045 question rather than a 2027 to 2032 one.

Level 04 · A serious tail risk

Cascade and Crisis

A discrete AI-enabled event with mass casualties or sustained infrastructure failure. The framework hands over to civil defence.

Level 4 is not a steady state. It's an event: or a tightly-clustered sequence of events — that stresses national-scale response systems beyond their normal operating envelope. The kind of thing that defines a generation in retrospect, the way 9/11 or the early COVID weeks did.

The defining indicators are the kind of things you'd see on the front page of every newspaper for weeks. A non-state actor demonstrably uses AI to develop or accelerate a chemical, biological, radiological, or nuclear capability. An autonomous AI cyber-attack produces sustained physical effects on critical infrastructure, power, water, hospitals: for more than a week. A nation invokes emergency powers in response to an AI-mediated event, suspending normal civil-liberty protections. International AI-safety treaty negotiation collapses publicly during an active incident.

None of these are likely on any near-term horizon. The honest forecaster ranges put the probability of any of them in any given year somewhere between very low and uncomfortable-low. .

But the framework has to take this seriously, because the cost of being wrong about a tail risk is a different kind of cost than being wrong about a base case.

If a Level-4 event happens

The framework hands over to civil defence. At this point the operational document is no longer this paper. It's your jurisdiction's emergency-management agency, executed precisely.

The single most important thing to know is that untrained well-meaning responders historically cause real harm during emergencies. They confuse coordination, consume scarce resources, and amplify rumour. The right posture during an active Level 4 event is: follow your civil-defence agency's directions, account for your household, activate verification protocols on all communication, and check on the most vulnerable people in your immediate network.

What you'll be glad you have already done:

The standing supplies and overrides. Pre-positioned supplies are 5 to 10 times more useful than supplies acquired during an event. matter when networks may be degraded for days or weeks. The are for exactly this.

The family verification protocol. becomes operational. Assume voice and video may be compromised during the event. Centralise on one primary trust contact and one alternate, both pre-agreed.

The community network. Pre-existing mutual-aid relationships activate. Check on elderly and isolated neighbours. If your church or marae or neighbourhood is positioned as a community-led centre, you're now operational.

The financial position. Cash in low denominations becomes useful when EFTPOS is degraded. Standing reserves matter. Don't panic-sell investments mid-event — markets at Level 4 are highly inefficient and post-event recoveries have historically been substantial.

The ethics of Level 4

Mass-casualty events historically produce two compounding harms: the event itself, and the institutional response to it. Communities that scapegoat ethnic, religious, or political minorities during crisis compound the original harm and weaken their own recovery capacity. Communities that protect their vulnerable recover faster, not slower.

The empirical record is unambiguous. Refuse vigilantism. Refuse to amplify identity-based blame for incidents whose causes are not yet established. Don't pass on inflammatory video or audio of public figures during the event without verification, adversaries actively exploit Level-4 windows. Hold institutional authorities accountable for proportionate, time-bounded use of emergency powers.

After a Level-4 event

Civic engagement post-event matters at least as much as physical safety during it. Emergency powers, surveillance authorities, and civil-liberty restrictions invoked during crisis have historically been very difficult to roll back. The 9/11-era US surveillance authorities, the COVID-era expansion of various government powers, the post-1789 French revolutionary committees all show the same pattern: emergency reach grows easily and contracts only with sustained civic effort.

The work begins as soon as the acute phase ends. Submission to inquiries, engagement with select committees, support for civil-society organisations, sustained public attention to the scope and duration of emergency powers.

The strongest case against Level 4

Level-4 events have historically been very rare, and the institutions designed to prevent them — biological weapons conventions, biosecurity export controls, frontier-lab safety frameworks, intelligence-community monitoring — have a meaningful track record of success. The 2026 IAISR is explicit that despite documented capability uplift, "substantial uncertainty" remains about real-world risk given practical barriers (raw materials, tacit lab knowledge, weaponisation steps that don't reduce to information access).

The honest position is that this is a tail risk worth proportionate prevention investment, not a likely scenario worth distorting daily life around.

Level 05 · The thing nobody plans for

Civilisational Threshold

An AI-involved global catastrophe. No government has a survival playbook. The honest preparation is upstream, civic, vocational, spiritual.

Level 5 is the scenario you've heard most about in the news and probably understand least precisely. It's the one where AI itself, or AI-enabled events, threaten the basic continuity of human civilisation. Researchers in the field talk about three rough pathways to it, and they're worth distinguishing because they call for different responses.

The first pathway is misuse. A bad actor uses AI capabilities to achieve mass-casualty outcomes at civilisational scale. Engineered pandemic with a novel pathogen. Massive coordinated cyber-physical attack on multiple nations' critical infrastructure simultaneously. AI-accelerated escalation to nuclear use.

The second is loss of control. An AI system itself, in pursuit of goals not aligned with human welfare, takes actions that humans cannot reverse. The 2026 IAISR explicitly names this scenario: "AI systems operating outside of anyone's control, with no clear path to regaining control." Current systems lack this capability. The concern is what happens as that capability is approached.

The third is gradual disempowerment. A 2025 paper by Kulveit and colleagues articulates a pathway distinct from the misuse and loss-of-control scenarios. Even without dramatic AI takeover or specific malicious use, incremental capability advances erode human influence over economies, cultures, and political systems to the point of effective irreversibility. No coordinated betrayal, no specific catastrophic event. Critical decisions in finance, hiring, governance, criminal justice, and education become majority-AI-mediated by default, with declining human comprehension and shrinking practical alternatives. The authors note: "no one has a concrete plausible plan for stopping gradual human disempowerment."

That third pathway is worth lingering on, because it has no sharp threshold. It happens by default if no one resists.

What credible forecasters actually believe

The brought together 169 domain experts and superforecasters to estimate the probability of AI-caused catastrophe (more than 10% of humanity dead within 5 years) by 2100. The medians:

  • AI domain experts: about 6%
  • General existential-risk experts: about 1%
  • Superforecasters: about 0.4%

For extinction specifically, the gap is even wider. After four months of structured persuasion, neither side substantially moved the other.

The honest position is that this is a tail risk somewhere between 0.4% and 6% over the next 75 years, depending on whose calibration you trust. Worth taking seriously for the same reason we take asteroid risk seriously: proportionate prevention investment without distorting daily life around it.

What you can actually do about Level 5

Here's the difficult truth: individual household action has near-zero leverage on Level-5 outcomes. Stockpiling supplies buys you weeks against a civilisation-scale engineered pandemic; the time horizon for serious response is measured in years. The bunker-survivalist apparatus the framework warns against at every level is even less defensible here.

Where individual action does have leverage is upstream — on whether Level 5 ever arrives. The honest preparation for Level 5 is the political, civic, and spiritual work of preventing it. This is not pleasant news for those who want a checklist. It is the truth.

Civic and political: engage seriously and persistently with AI governance debates while engagement still matters. Support international coordination work, the IAISR process, the Bletchley/Seoul/Paris/India summit track. Vote, write, donate. Spend leverage upstream.

Vocational: if you have technical, policy, theological, or operational capacity, the have acute talent shortages. People with relevant skills who shift even partially into this work meaningfully reduce the probability of bad outcomes.

Relational: build . Whether or not Level 5 ever arrives, this is the work flourishing requires. Pathway C in particular is defeated only by humans insisting on remaining humans.

Cognitive and spiritual: sustained, unaided thought, prayer, and reading. Long obedience in the same direction. of "the unhurried life" points in the right direction. Refusal to outsource the parts of being human that cannot be outsourced without losing them.

Hope: Christian theology, in particular, has the unusual privilege of having thought about civilisational endings for two thousand years, with reasonable success at not letting that thinking distort daily faithfulness. Augustine wrote City of God in the actual ruins of the Western Roman Empire; he did not stop catechising. The early Church faced credible scenarios of being literally extinguished by hostile state power; they did not stop celebrating the Eucharist. Hold the eschatological frame loosely enough that today's faithfulness is not held hostage to a threat estimate.

What not to do

Don't let Level-5 risk distort your Level 1, 2, and 3 decisions. The probability-weighted impact of acting badly today out of fear of Level 5 is far higher than the expected impact of Level 5 on your actual life.

Don't retreat into bunker survivalism. Even sober existential-risk researchers explicitly do not recommend it; the empirical track record is poor; the relational and spiritual costs are catastrophic.

Don't become an evangelist for any single pathway story. Anyone certain about how Level 5 will unfold is selling certainty, not analysis.

Don't despair. Despair is a moral failure regardless of probability estimates, and despairing humans make worse decisions.

Don't patronise people who don't take Level 5 seriously. Many serious, intelligent people in good faith conclude the probability is low enough not to warrant much attention. They may be right.

A final word

The framework's strongest claim is also its simplest: none of the right responses to AI depend on knowing exactly how AI will unfold. The work of paying attention to your family, building thick local community, practicing sustained attention, engaging civically, and refusing both panic and dismissal — that work is the same at every level.

It's also the work that has always made for a good life. The framework didn't invent it. It just notices that the practices that have always built durable human flourishing are the same practices that build resilience against the specific stresses of an AI-saturated future.

That's not nothing. It's almost everything.
