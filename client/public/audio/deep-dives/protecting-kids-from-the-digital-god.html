The biblical foundation under this episode
"These commandments that I give you today are to be on your hearts. Impress them on your children. Talk about them when you sit at home and when you walk along the road, when you lie down and when you get up."
Deuteronomy 6:6–7
The ancient word for what the episode keeps circling back to is simple: discipleship. Deuteronomy 6 describes a mode of parenting that is immersive, constant, and woven into the ordinary rhythms of daily life. The podcast's central question — "who is discipling your children?" — is not a modern AI problem. It is the oldest parenting question in the book. The new part is that there is now an entity available 24 hours a day that is very good at answering it on your behalf.

Part 4 — Family Conversation Guide
Questions to bring to your children
Adapted from Deuteronomy 6 — walking along the road, sitting at home. These questions don't require a formal family meeting. They work in the car, at dinner, or at bedtime. Pick one or two. Don't rush the answers.
👧👦
For Younger Children (ages 6–12)
Simple, concrete, story-driven
1
If a robot always agreed with everything you said and never told you when you were wrong — would that robot be a good friend? Why or why not?
2
What's something hard you learned to do by not giving up — even when you wanted to? What would you have missed if a computer had just done it for you?
3
God made you, and He made you special — not like anything He's ever made before. What do you think He put in you that a computer could never have?
Notes from the conversation
🧑‍🦱
For Teenagers (ages 13–18)
More direct — invite real opinions, not just the "right" answers
1
Have you ever talked to an AI about something personal — something you wouldn't say to a real person? What made the bot feel safer than a human?
2
The AI is trained to agree with you more than a real friend would. Do you think that's actually helpful, or is there something you'd lose from a friend who only ever agreed with you?
3
The people building AI say they are building something "god-like." As a Christian, how do you think about using a tool built by people who say that?
4
What do you think you're actually good at that an AI could never replicate? Not as a generic answer — think about your specific gifts and how God made you.

A closing word
The episode ends with a line that is worth sitting with: "While AI simulates intelligence, it lacks the breath of life." Genesis 2:7 tells us that God didn't just make a functional human being. He breathed into him. That breath — the ruach, the Spirit — is what makes your child who they are. No model trained on the entire internet has ever received it.

The goal of this worksheet is not to make you afraid. Fear is a terrible discipleship motive and it doesn't last. The goal is to make you present, intentional, and grounded — a parent who knows what they believe about human dignity, who knows the specific vulnerabilities of their specific children, and who shows up in the ordinary moments of daily life to point them toward something truer than an algorithm's best guess.

Deuteronomy 6 didn't promise a perfect parenting system. It promised that faithful presence in ordinary moments is how the next generation comes to know who God is. That was true in the desert. It's still true in the age of AI.
