## 0. Design principles for the whole kit

- **Short by design.** A policy people actually read beats a comprehensive one they
  don't. The core template is ~2 pages.
- **Convictions before rules.** State what the organisation believes first; the
  rules follow from it.
- **Their voice, not ours.** `[BRACKETED PROMPTS]` invite the leader to write the
  framing themselves. We supply structure and a reference draft, never a finished
  identity.
- **Plain language.** No jargon the reader has to look up. The glossary is optional.
- **One decision rule they can remember:** *When in doubt, leave it out.*

## 1. The five convictions (sector-neutral)

These are the load-bearing values. Every sector flavour keeps all five; church /
faith flavours may re-title them in theological language (see `sectors/church.md`).

1. **Human judgment is irreplaceable.** AI assists people; it does not replace human
   judgment, relationships, or responsibility. The final decision, and the
   accountability for it, stays with a person.
2. **People before efficiency.** We will not use AI in a way that degrades the
   quality of human attention we owe the people we serve.
3. **Truth and verification.** We verify what AI produces before we rely on it, and
   we never present AI-generated work as something it is not. AI can be confidently
   wrong ("hallucination"); the human who uses it owns the result.
4. **Privacy and confidentiality.** Personal and confidential information about the
   people we serve does not go into public AI tools. This is both an ethical
   commitment and a requirement of the NZ Privacy Act 2020.
5. **Shared accountability.** AI use is not a private matter each person navigates
   alone. It is held together — set by leadership, owned by everyone.

## 2. The policy body

### Scope
Applies to all paid staff, volunteers in significant roles, and anyone acting on
behalf of `[ORGANISATION]` in an official capacity. Covers AI use in communications,
administration, content creation, service delivery, and decision support.

### What we welcome (green-light uses)
- Drafting and researching communications, documents, and content — where a person
  reviews and approves the final output before it goes out in `[ORGANISATION]`'s name.
- Administrative tasks: summarising notes, organising information, drafting
  correspondence — where no confidential personal data is involved.
- Translation and language support.
- Learning, brainstorming, and exploring ideas.

### What we do not do (red-light uses)
- Enter confidential or personal information about the people we serve into any
  public AI tool.
- Present AI-generated content as the original, unassisted work of a person.
- Use AI to replace the human care, presence, or judgment that is central to our work.
- Make significant decisions about people based solely on AI-generated analysis.
- Use AI tools that have not been approved for `[ORGANISATION]` use.

### What we are honest about (disclosure standard)
When AI meaningfully helped produce something, we say so; a brief acknowledgement is
enough. **The test:** if someone would feel misled to learn AI was used, we should
have been more transparent.

### Privacy and data
`[ORGANISATION]` holds personal information under the NZ Privacy Act 2020. Anything
typed into a public AI tool leaves our systems and enters the systems of large
technology companies. Personal details and confidential information must never be
processed this way. **When in doubt, leave it out.**

### Who is responsible
`[LEADERSHIP BODY]` holds collective responsibility for this policy. Every person is
personally responsible for their own AI use within it. If unsure, ask before you
act — we would rather have the conversation than discover the problem afterward.

## 3. Quick reference — five questions (for the one-pager and template)

1. Does this involve personal or confidential information about anyone we serve? → If
   yes, do not use AI.
2. Am I comfortable being transparent about AI's role here, if asked? → If not,
   rethink or disclose.
3. Have I verified the content for accuracy and appropriateness? → If not, do that first.
4. Is this replacing something that deserves human attention? → If yes, choose the person.
5. Has this tool been approved for our use? → If not, check first.

## 4. Traffic Light — data categories

| Light | Meaning | Examples |
|-------|---------|----------|
| 🟢 **Green — safe** | General, non-personal, already-public information | Public info, general research, admin drafting with no names, marketing copy for review |
| 🟡 **Amber — caution** | Internal but not sensitive; de-identify first | Internal drafts, meeting summaries with names removed, aggregated non-personal data |
| 🔴 **Red — never** | Personal, confidential, or legally protected information | Names + personal details, financial records, health/pastoral/counselling notes, HR files, anything shared in confidence |

## 4b. Traffic Light — tools (governance tiers)

| Light | Tier | Rule |
|-------|------|------|
| 🟢 **Green** | Approved | On our Approved AI Tools list. Use within these guidelines. |
| 🟡 **Amber** | Provisional | Under trial by staff only. No personal data. Not yet for frontline/service use. |
| 🔴 **Red** | Unapproved | Not assessed or failed assessment. Do not use for `[ORGANISATION]` work. Any tool that can't show NZ Privacy Act 2020 compliance sits here by default. |

## 7. Acknowledgement clauses (for the form)

Each person confirms they will:
- Use only approved AI tools for official `[ORGANISATION]` activities.
- Never enter personal or confidential information about the people we serve into any
  public AI tool.
- Be transparent about AI use in content they produce for `[ORGANISATION]`.
- Verify AI-generated content for accuracy before using it.
- Take personal responsibility for all content they produce, AI-assisted or not.
- Raise concerns about AI use with leadership.
