The Framework
Automation vs. Augmentation — why this is the only question that matters
Stanford Digital Economy Lab — August 2025
The negative employment impacts from AI are concentrated in roles where AI automates tasks. In occupations where AI augments human work, employment for workers remains stable or increases. Workers who use AI to learn new skills see employment growth. Those who use it only to automate existing tasks are losing ground.
Erik Brynjolfsson et al., "Canaries in the Coal Mine" — Stanford, ADP administrative payroll data, millions of workers
A Christian foundation for this exercise
"The Lord God took the man and put him in the Garden of Eden to work it and take care of it."
Genesis 2:15
This was before the fall — work wasn't a consequence of sin, it was part of God's original design for human flourishing. When we ask which parts of our work AI can't replace, we're ultimately asking: what does it mean to be made in God's image? Presence, conscience, relationship, moral accountability — these aren't just career assets. They're expressions of the imago Dei that no model trained on data can replicate.
Automation risk
AI replaces the task
You hand the work over entirely. AI does it adequately. Your role in that specific task shrinks or disappears.
→
Retrieving information you then present to others
→
Producing first drafts of routine documents
→
Scheduling, sorting, routing, categorising

→
Answering known questions with known answers
vs
Augmentation opportunity
AI strengthens the task
You use AI to do it faster or better — but the judgement, relationship, or expertise is still yours. Your value increases.
✓
Judgement calls where context, ethics, or relationships matter
✓
Trust-based relationships that depend on knowing and being known
✓
Novel problems that don't match any existing template
✓
Accountability — situations where someone must own the outcome
✓
Physical presence or embodied skill AI cannot replicate

A closing thought
The anxiety most professionals feel about AI isn't irrational. It's just pointed at the wrong thing. The real risk isn't that AI is coming for your job — it's that you spend the next five years defending tasks that were never what made you good at your job in the first place, while neglecting the things that make you genuinely irreplaceable.

The Stanford research is clear: workers who use AI to learn and grow see employment growth. Workers who use it only to automate existing tasks are losing ground. The question was never whether AI is useful. The question is whether you're using this moment of disruption to get clearer about where your real value lies — and whether the people around you can see it.

For those of us who follow Jesus, there's something deeper underneath all of this. Work was given to us before the fall — as an act of stewardship, of care, of creative engagement with the world God made. When you protect and develop the things that require presence, conscience, and genuine relationship, you're not just protecting your career. You're being faithful to the person God made you to be.
