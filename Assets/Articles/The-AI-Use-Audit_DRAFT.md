# You Approved the Budget. Nobody Approved the Tools.

**Nathaniel Baldock** · 6 min read · June 2026

---

Last month a school principal told me, with genuine confidence, that her staff weren't really using AI yet. They were waiting for a policy. They were being careful.

Then we ran a ten-minute audit. ChatGPT on personal accounts. Grammarly in every browser. Otter joining meetings nobody remembered inviting. Canva generating newsletter graphics with the school crest pasted in. None of it on the IT spreadsheet. None of it on a plan that excluded customer data from model training.

She wasn't negligent. She was normal.

That gap — between what leadership thinks is happening and what is actually happening on screens — is now the most common AI problem I see in businesses, churches, and schools. Not rogue superintelligence. Not job loss at scale. Just tools, already in daily use, with nobody quite sure where the information goes after someone hits send.

---

## The question behind the policy panic

Every organisation I work with is stuck in the same loop. Someone reads an article about AI risk. Someone else reads one about AI opportunity. A working group is formed. A policy draft appears. It sits in a shared drive while staff quietly keep doing what works.

The policy panic isn't stupid. It's just answering the wrong question first.

The question isn't *Should we have an AI policy?* You already need one. The question is *What are we actually doing right now — tool by tool, account by account — and which of those things need a small adjustment versus a hard stop?*

That is a much less glamorous problem. It is also the one you can solve this week.

## What a useful audit actually asks

The AI Use Audit I built for clients takes about ten minutes. No jargon. No trick questions. No tech knowledge required. You tick the tools your organisation uses — including the ones people reach for on personal accounts, which is where the interesting findings usually live — and answer three questions for each one.

**Which plan are you on?** This matters more than most vendors admit. ChatGPT Plus at twenty dollars a month sits on the same consumer privacy terms as the free tier: your conversations can still be used to train OpenAI's models unless someone has toggled the right setting. Paying does not automatically mean safe. The business tier — ChatGPT Team, Microsoft 365 Copilot, Gemini for Workspace — is a different product in privacy terms, not just a bigger allowance.

**What kind of information goes in?** Public stuff — drafts, ideas, general research. Internal business information — strategy notes, operations plans, financial models. Customer or staff personal details — names, orders, health information, HR records. The same tool can be green for one use and red for another.

**Does someone review the output before it goes out?** Always. Usually. Often not. That last answer, combined with sensitive data, is where I see the most avoidable harm: AI-generated client emails, meeting summaries, or CRM notes going straight out the door because the tool made them sound confident.

Those three answers, crossed with what each vendor's policy actually says for that specific plan, produce a simple colour code: **green** (good to go), **amber** (check this), **red** (act now or review settings). Not a moral verdict on AI. A prioritised to-do list.

## The surprises that keep showing up

After dozens of these audits across trades businesses, churches, schools, and nonprofits, a few patterns repeat.

**The paid-personal-account trap.** Someone upgraded to ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro on a company card and assumed that meant enterprise privacy. It usually doesn't. Consumer tiers train on your data by default across most major assistants — and the opt-out, where one exists, only protects conversations from that point forward. Anything already submitted may already be in the training pipeline.

**The meeting bot nobody remembers inviting.** Otter, Fathom, Fireflies, Zoom AI Companion — transcription tools spread fast because they genuinely save time. They also record voices, names, and confidential discussions. On consumer plans, some of that data is used for model improvement by default. The operational risk isn't only privacy; it's consent. A bot joining a client call without explicit agreement is a liability question before it is a technology question.

**The creative tool that publishes your work.** This one catches people off guard every time: on Midjourney's basic and standard plans, prompts and generated images can be **public on midjourney.com by default** — even work created in a private Discord channel. There is no safe configuration for proprietary or client-confidential visual assets on those tiers. Stealth Mode on higher plans hides outputs from the public gallery but does not stop training on your prompts.

**The helpful browser extension.** Grammarly, Copilot in Edge, Gemini in Chrome — AI woven into tools people already use. Convenient. Also easy to forget that the text being polished may include client details, pastoral notes, or student information passing through a consumer account's training defaults.

None of this means ban AI. It means know which configuration you're actually running, because *the tool name on the invoice* and *the privacy posture of the account your staff actually use* are often two different things.

## Shadow AI is the whole game

The audit explicitly asks you to tick tools used on **personal accounts**. That instruction makes some owners uncomfortable. It shouldn't.

Shadow AI — staff using capable tools without telling IT, without a business plan, without a review step — isn't rebellion. It's what happens when the official pathway is slow and the unofficial one saves an hour on a Tuesday.

The goal isn't to catch people out. It's to see the full picture so you can support what's working and fix what's exposed. Some of the best audit findings are green: a team member using NotebookLM brilliantly for research, or Granola saving a sales lead three hours of notes — tools worth keeping and formalising.

Optional team surveys make that picture fuller. Anonymous five-minute parallel surveys, with individual responses never shown to the owner, reveal tools leadership didn't know about and use cases worth amplifying. Most owners do the audit alone first. The team step is there when you're ready for depth, not as a requirement.

## Start with one honest hour

If you're a business owner, school leader, or pastor reading this and recognising your organisation, here's the practical sequence.

Block ten minutes. Go to the audit with an honest list of tools — don't skip the personal accounts. Answer the three questions per tool without optimising for the answer you wish were true. Read the red and amber cards first; they're usually quick fixes — a toggle, a plan upgrade, a review step — not a rebuild.

Then pick one policy conversation for this month: what may go into consumer AI accounts, what must stay in business-tier tools, and who checks output before it reaches a client, parent, or congregation member.

You don't need to solve AI in one sitting. You need to stop guessing.
