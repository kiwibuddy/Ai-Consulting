A Christian foundation for this exercise
"Build houses and settle down; plant gardens and eat what they produce... seek the peace and prosperity of the city... because if it prospers, you too will prosper."
Jeremiah 29:5–7
Jeremiah wrote this to exiles in Babylon — people in a foreign, uncertain, uncomfortable situation who had no idea what the next decade held. God's instruction wasn't "wait for things to stabilise." It was build, plant, invest, pray. Not because the future was secure, but because faithful people act in the present regardless. That's a different posture than either anxious paralysis or reckless optimism. It's long-horizon investment in uncertain conditions — which is exactly what this worksheet is about.
Why linear planning fails in uncertain environments
Stanford researchers found that the gap between workers whose employment grew and those whose employment declined wasn't about which industry they were in — it was about whether their specific contributions were augmentation-based or automation-based. That means the useful question isn't "what will my industry look like?" It's "what contributions can I build that hold value across multiple possible futures?"
Stanford Digital Economy Lab — "Canaries in the Coal Mine," August 2025

This worksheet does not ask you to predict what your industry will look like in 2031. It asks you to map three plausible scenarios — conservative, significant, and radical — and then identify the moves that pay off in all three. Those multi-scenario moves are your actual strategy. Everything else is a bet on a specific future that may or may not arrive.

Part 5 — Sit with these
The questions no LinkedIn post will ask you
1
Which scenario do you hope is true — and is that hope influencing your planning?
Most career plans are secretly bets on the scenario we prefer. Name yours — then check whether you're planning honestly or hopefully.
2
If Scenario C arrived in two years — radical disruption to your industry — what would you actually do?
Not what you'd hope to do. What you'd realistically be positioned to do, based on where you are today.
3
What move have you been putting off for two years that, if you're honest, appears in your payoff matrix?
Most people already know what their best career move is. The question is why they haven't made it yet.
4
In five years, what do you want to be able to say you did during this moment of disruption — when it was uncertain and uncomfortable and most people were just hoping it would stabilise?
This is the question that separates people who use uncertainty as an excuse from those who use it as a catalyst.
5
What would it mean to pursue this five-year plan with your hands open rather than clenched — to build, plant, and work faithfully, while holding the outcomes loosely?
The Jeremiah exiles didn't know how long they'd be in Babylon. They built anyway. Faithful investment and surrendered outcomes aren't opposites — for a Christian, they belong together.
A final word — and a challenge
Nobody is going to hand you a map of what the next five years looks like. The forecasters disagree. The companies building this technology are genuinely uncertain. The economists are divided. You are operating in genuine uncertainty — and the honest response to genuine uncertainty is not a perfect plan. It's a robust one.

A robust strategy is one built on moves that pay off in multiple futures. Skills that hold value whether AI disrupts modestly or radically. Relationships that survive restructures. Contributions so deeply relational and contextual that they remain valuable whatever automation arrives next.

You have now done the analysis that most people avoid for years. The question is what you do with it in the next 30 days. Pick one move. Start it this week. Tell someone about it so the accountability is real. The uncertainty will not resolve before you act — which means the action comes first.

For those who follow Jesus: Jeremiah told the exiles to build and plant in a place that felt temporary, hostile, and out of their control. Not because it was safe, but because faithfulness doesn't wait for certainty before it gets to work. Build something. Plant something. Seek the good of the place you're in. Hold the outcomes with open hands.
