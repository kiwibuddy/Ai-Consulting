The observation that changes everything
"The social internet is a profoundly spiritual and liturgical environment that trains our hearts to desire a particular vision of the good life."
Samuel James — Digital Liturgies: Rediscovering Christian Wisdom in an Online Age (2023)
"
"I no longer know if I like something because it's me, or because an algorithm suggested it to me."
Research participant — RSIS International study on consumer fatigue with AI, 2025

A liturgy is a set of repeated practices that shapes what you love — not what you think you believe, but what your body, attention, and desires actually reach for. Sunday worship is a liturgy. So is the Rosary. So is the way you wake up, reach for your phone, check your feeds, scroll through your notifications, and start your day before you've said a word to God. Both are forming you. The question is which one is winning.

Part 4
The Questions That Won't Let You Off
Don't rush. These are worth sitting with for a while before you write. The first answer is rarely the real one.
1
If someone watched how you actually spent your attention over the past week, what would they conclude you love most?
Not what you say you love. What your time reveals.
2
When did you last sit in silence with God for more than five minutes — without a phone nearby?
What did that feel like? What does your answer reveal?
3
What do you believe about yourself after a heavy session of scrolling? Is that true?
The algorithm has an opinion about who you are. So does God. Which voice is louder right now?
4
What spiritual practice has been quietly displaced by digital habits in the past year?
Prayer? Scripture? Sabbath? Silence? Be specific about what used to be there.
5
If you genuinely believed that 23 minutes of focus are lost every time you check your phone, what would you do differently today?
Research finding, not anecdote. What would it mean for your prayer life, your Bible reading, your relationships?

Counter-Liturgies — What to Put in Its Place
The answer to a bad liturgy isn't willpower — it's a better liturgy. Dallas Willard called these the spiritual disciplines: the practices through which grace flows and desire is recalibrated. Pick one or two to begin this week.
Morning prayer before the phone

A closing word
The goal of this worksheet isn't to make you feel bad about your screen use. It's to make the invisible visible — because you can't respond to something you haven't honestly looked at.

Samuel James is right that the internet is a liturgical environment. It is shaping your desires, your beliefs, your loves — slowly, below the level of conscious choice, through sheer repetition. That is not a metaphor. It's how formation works. It's how the mall, the military, the monastery all work too.

The good news is that the same mechanism that shapes us toward distraction can be redirected. God is not in competition with the algorithm for your attention. He is simply, patiently, asking you to bring your actual self — the distracted, divided, over-scrolled version of you — back to him. Over and over. And in that returning, you are changed. Not by willpower. By grace, mediated through practice.
